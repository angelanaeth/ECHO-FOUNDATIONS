Foundations Manual

The intro-level coaching playbook for new EchoDevo coaches — before you graduate to the full ECHO Manual.

Welcome

Welcome to the EchoDevo Foundations Manual.

This manual is the entry point into the EchoDevo coaching system. It exists to bring new coaches up to speed on the language, the zones, the testing protocols, the block types, and the coaching philosophy that the full ECHO Coaching Manual is built on.

Foundations is intentionally simpler than ECHO. ECHO is a complete decision-making framework — it teaches coaches how to diagnose athletes, select training blocks, dose stress, and translate decisions into clear athlete communication. Foundations teaches the vocabulary and the foundational physiology that has to be in place before any of that decision-making makes sense.

Read this manual first. Then graduate to ECHO when you're ready.

How to Use This Manual

Foundations is organized in the order a new coach should learn the system:

  • Zones — the language of intensity. HR, power, pace, RPE, and how they connect.
  • Testing — CP and CS — how we establish an athlete's actual sustainable threshold instead of guessing.
  • Block Types — the building blocks of any training plan.
  • Fueling & Hydration — the Power of 3, the sweat test, and the basics of carbohydrate.
  • Mental Foundations — the mindset skills that determine whether physical fitness translates into performance.

Each section can be read on its own, but the manual is designed to be read in order. The zones don't make sense without testing, the blocks don't make sense without zones, and the fueling and mindset sections amplify everything else.

The Two Foundational Rules

Before any of the technical content matters, two principles sit underneath all of it.

1. Personalization — Train YOUR body, not a generic plan.
Every athlete has unique physiology, life context, training history, and response to load. A workout that builds one athlete will break another. The numbers we set — heart rate zones, power zones, paces, fueling targets — are derived from that specific athlete's testing and history, not from a chart in a magazine.

2. Use data and principles to make decisions.
Coaching is not guessing. We anchor every training and pacing decision in objective signals: Critical Power, Critical Speed, heart rate response, perceived exertion, and how the athlete is actually adapting week to week. Vibes are not a plan.

These two rules pull in opposite directions on purpose. Personalization keeps us from rigid template-coaching. Data keeps us from drifting into "whatever feels right." Holding both at the same time is what separates coaching from cheerleading.

Who Foundations Is For

  • Anyone who wants to understand how endurance training actually works before diving into the full ECHO framework.

Zones

Zones are the language of training. They turn a vague instruction like "go easy" or "push it" into something specific, measurable, and repeatable. Every workout in your plan will be prescribed in zones — so the goal of this section is simple: by the end of it, you should know what each zone means, how to find it, and how to know you're in it.

ECHO uses one shared 5-band system across every modality: ZR · Z1 · Z2 · Z3 · VO2 Max. The bands stay the same whether you're talking about heart rate, bike power, run pace, or swim pace — what changes is the anchor each modality is built from. This shared vocabulary is intentional: when your coach says "Z2 ride" or "Z3 run repeats," you should be able to hold those two ideas in the same shape in your head.

The four ways to measure effort

  • Heart rate (HR) — internal cost. Universal. Slightly delayed and influenced by heat, sleep, hydration, and stress.
  • Power (bike) — external output, instantaneous, no lag. The cleanest bike anchor when available.
  • Pace (run, swim) — external output expressed as time-per-distance. The cleanest run and swim anchor.
  • RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion) — subjective effort. Not a primary zone system in ECHO — it's the verification and safety-valve. If the output target says 250W but RPE says 10/10 on a Z2 day, the day is wrong, not the math.

You don't need all four to train well. The rule we work from is Anchor → Range → Verify: test the output anchor (CP for bike, CS for run and swim), build the zone ranges from that anchor, and verify internal cost with HR, breathing, and RPE. Then apply context — heat, terrain, fatigue, fueling — on top.

The bands (what they're for)

  • ZR — Recovery. Active regeneration, reset work, low-cost movement. Unlimited duration. If you finish a ZR session feeling worked, it wasn't ZR.
  • Z1 — Durability Base. Primary low-cost volume; chassis-building. Easy steady efforts in the 2-6+ hour range on the bike. This is where the aerobic engine and the durability foundation are built. The majority of your training year lives in ZR-Z1.
  • Z2 — Steady / Durable. Race-pace feel for long-course racing. Sustained aerobic work, race-adjacent control, 1-3 hours on the bike. Sub-threshold but real work — talking shrinks to short phrases.
  • Z3 — Threshold / Critical. Hard sustained efforts at or near threshold. 20-90 minutes total of work, usually delivered as intervals. This is where you train sustainable expression directly — the ceiling of "sustainable hard."
  • VO2 Max — Peak / Ceiling. Short maximal-aerobic intervals, typically 3-8 minutes. Builds the ceiling, not the floor. High cost, low repeatability, and only useful when the structure underneath it exists.

HR Zones — % of LTHR

LTHR is your Lactate Threshold Heart Rate: the heart rate you can hold for roughly one hour of all-out effort. It's not your max HR. Once we know your LTHR, every HR zone is a percentage of that number. (How we test for LTHR lives in Section 3.)

Canonical reference (live from ECHO): these values are auto-synced. Do not edit by hand.
  • ZR — Recovery — ≤ 76% of LTHR
  • Z1 — Durability Base — 76-86% of LTHR
  • Z2 — Steady / Durable — 86-93% of LTHR
  • Z3 — Threshold / Critical — 93-100% of LTHR
  • VO2 Max — HR is a lagging confirmation only. HR cannot reliably govern VO2 work because it ramps too slowly to catch short intervals. Use power or pace, with RPE as the cross-check.

Bike Power Zones — % of CP

CP (Critical Power) is the highest power you can sustain in a quasi-steady state — the horizontal asymptote of your power-duration curve. CP is the bike anchor for every zone, every interval target, and every pacing decision. Section 3 covers the 3 / 6 / 12-minute test we use to find it.

Canonical reference (live from ECHO):
  • ZR — Recovery — < 65% of CP · unlimited duration
  • Z1 — Durability Base — 65-79% of CP · 2-6+ hour efforts
  • Z2 — Steady / Durable — 79-89% of CP · 1-3 hours
  • Z3 — Threshold / Critical — 89-100% of CP · 20-90 min
  • VO2 Max — 100-120% of CP · 3-8 min intervals

Power is instantaneous — it tells you what you're doing right now, with no lag. HR tells you what your body is responding to, which is delayed by 30-60 seconds. Use both. When power and HR disagree, treat it as information — not a problem to solve. Heat, dehydration, poor sleep, or accumulated fatigue all show up as HR running high (or sometimes low) for a given power output. The right move depends on which signal the day belongs to.

Run Zones — % of CS

CS (Critical Speed) is the running analog of CP — the highest pace you can sustain in a quasi-steady state. CS is the run anchor, and pace is the cleanest field expression of sustainable run performance. Run zones are built from CS, and HR is used to verify the cost.

Canonical reference (live from ECHO):
  • ZR — Recovery — slower than 75% of CS pace · HR ≤ 76% LTHR · regeneration and reset
  • Z1 — Durability Base — 75-85% of CS pace · HR ~76-86% LTHR · primary low-cost run volume
  • Z2 — Steady / Durable — 85-95% of CS pace · HR 86-93% LTHR · steady, durability support, race-adjacent
  • Z3 — Threshold / Critical — 95-105% of CS pace · HR 93-100% LTHR · threshold / critical-speed work
  • VO2 Max — 105-120% of CS pace · HR lagging confirmation only · 3-8 min interval work

Reminder: run pace is time-per-distance (e.g. 4:30/km), so higher % of CS pace = slower pace. Only VO2 Max is faster than CS itself.

Run power. If you train with a run power meter, run power should mirror CS, not replace it. CS is the field-tested reality; watts help you execute that reality on hills and uneven terrain. When watts and CS disagree, trust the field-tested CS anchor first — then use watts as a secondary execution aid. On hot days, hills, or off-road, HR and RPE will be more honest than pace.

Swim Zones — % of CS (swim)

Swim CS is the pool analog of CP and run CS — the highest swim pace you can hold in a quasi-steady state. Testing protocols (multiple distances, e.g. 200 / 400 / 800 or 200 / 400 / 1500) live in Section 3.

Swim zones compress around CS more tightly than bike or run because of how sensitive swimming is to technique. The percentage bands look small — that's correct.

Canonical reference (live from ECHO):
  • ZR — Recovery — 90-95% of CS · unlimited duration · easy technique work
  • Z1 — Durability Base — 95-97% of CS · 45-90 min · long aerobic swim volume
  • Z2 — Steady / Durable — 97-99% of CS · 30-60 min · sustained aerobic, race-pace for long course
  • Z3 — Threshold / Critical — 99-102% of CS · 20-40 min · threshold / critical-speed swim work
  • Above CS — intervals only:
    • Repeatable: CS + 5% — e.g. 6-8 × 200m
    • Short max: CS + 10% — e.g. 4-8 × 25-100m

Hold the Water

Swim intensity is governed by technique before it is governed by effort. You can muscle through a hard bike or run set — you cannot muscle through swim intensity without losing the catch, the rhythm, and the energy economy that make swimming work.

The phrase we use is Hold the Water. Feel the pressure on your forearm and palm through the entire pull, on every stroke. If a set forces you to lose your hold, the prescribed intensity was too high for that day — slow down and keep the technique intact. Speed in the water comes from a longer, cleaner stroke, not a more violent one.

This is why swim zones compress around CS the way they do, and why above-CS work is delivered as discrete intervals rather than sustained efforts. Hold the water first, then add pressure.

RPE — the verification and safety valve

RPE is not a primary zone system in ECHO. It's the verification layer: a way to check whether the prescribed output is actually affordable for the athlete on the day.

The rule: RPE outranks the anchor when internal strain spikes. If the output target says 250W and the athlete reports RPE 10/10 on what should be a Z2 day, the day is wrong, not the math. Pull back the output, finish the session at an honest cost, and figure out the why afterward (heat? illness? sleep debt? fueling? life stress?).

That's the safety-valve job RPE does. Every athlete should be in the habit of rating sessions in plain language — "easy," "steady," "hard," "very hard," "maxed out" — and reporting it. The number itself matters less than the trend over time relative to the prescribed work.

How they fit together — Anchor → Range → Verify

  • 1. Anchor — test the output anchor (CP for bike, CS for run and swim, LTHR for HR). Use the best anchor available; fallbacks only when a real test isn't possible.
  • 2. Range — build ZR / Z1 / Z2 / Z3 / VO2 Max from the anchor. Do not blur the edges to match athlete preference.
  • 3. Verify — check internal cost with HR drift, breathing, and RPE. If the verification doesn't match the anchor, that's information.
  • 4. Apply context — heat, terrain, fatigue, and fueling can override. Zones are anchors, not permission to ignore reality.

The ECHO Coaching Manual layers in advanced decision logic on top of these zones — when to push, when to hold, when to back off, when to layer durability work, how to interpret signals across an entire training block. Foundations gives you the vocabulary; ECHO teaches you to coach with it.

Questions on any zone? Ask your EchoDevo coach, or use the chat widget at the bottom-right.

Testing — CP, CS, and LTHR

Zones only mean something if the anchor underneath them is real. If CP, CS, or LTHR is wrong, every zone is wrong — and every block decision built on those zones drifts with them. That's why testing is its own section, not a footnote.

The goal of this section is to give you the three field protocols ECHO actually uses, the rules for when to retest, and the rules for when not to retest.

The principle — Anchor first

ECHO is built on three field-tested anchors: CP for the bike, CS for the run, CS (swim) for the pool, and LTHR as the universal HR anchor. Every zone, every interval target, every race-pace number, and every block decision in ECHO flows downstream from those four numbers.

If they are wrong, every downstream prescription is wrong. If they are stale, the system drifts. If they are defended — kept current on a sensible cadence, never inflated to flatter the athlete — the coaching decisions on top of them become defensible, repeatable, and productive.

Bike — Critical Power (CP)

What CP is. The horizontal asymptote of your power-duration curve — the highest power you can sustain in a quasi-steady state. CP is not FTP. FTP is a single 60-minute estimate; CP comes from multiple maximal efforts that expose how your output actually changes with duration. CP is also why we use three test points and not two: with three points, a poorly paced trial shows up as error in the fit. With two points, every line looks perfect — there is no way to detect a bad trial.

The 3 / 6 / 12 protocol. Three maximal efforts on the bike:

  • 3-minute maximal effort — the shortest and most violent. The athlete rides as hard as possible for the full three minutes. The average power from this effort also serves as a proxy for the power at VO2 max.
  • 6-minute maximal effort — sits in the upper severe domain. Draws heavily on both aerobic and anaerobic capacity; provides the second point on the curve.
  • 12-minute maximal effort — the longest trial, sitting closest to CP itself. Anchors the lower end of the curve and is the most durable of the three.

Execution. Tests can be performed on separate days, or on the same day in descending order of duration (12 → 6 → 3) with 30 minutes of active recovery between efforts. Each effort must be truly maximal — the athlete should finish knowing that continuing for even 10 more seconds at that power would not have been possible. A thorough warm-up of 15-20 minutes including progressive builds and one short hard opener is required before the first test of any session.

What you do with the numbers. Plot total work (W = power × time, in joules) against time. Three points. The slope of the linear regression is CP. The y-intercept is W′ (the finite reserve above CP). The math can be done in a spreadsheet, in WKO5, Golden Cheetah, or intervals.icu — Foundations doesn't require you to do it by hand. ECHO covers the deeper interpretation.

FTP fallback. When a clean three-point test isn't available, the standard FTP fallbacks (0.95 of a 20-min effort, or 0.9 of the average of two 8-min efforts) can be used. Treat the result as approximate, not as a substitute for CP.

Run — Critical Speed (CS)

What CS is. The running analog of CP — the highest pace you can sustain in a quasi-steady state. CS is the asymptote of your pace-vs-duration curve. Run zones are built from CS, and run power (if you train with it) is anchored back to CS rather than guessed from a device default.

The protocol. Multi-distance maximal time trials on flat, measured ground. ECHO documents two acceptable shapes:

  • 3K + 10K time trials on flat terrain — the practical option for most athletes. Two well-paced maximal efforts on separate days.
  • 400 / 800 / 3200 m on a track for tighter curve-fitting. The 400 m sits high on the curve (proxy for sVO2 max), the 800 m sits in the severe domain, and the 3200 m anchors the bottom of the curve near CS itself.

Execution. Track preferred for the 400/800/3200 version. Each effort must be truly maximal. Warm up with 15-20 minutes of easy running followed by 3-4 strides. If running multiple trials on the same day, give substantial recovery between them; otherwise separate days are cleaner.

What you do with the numbers. Convert each distance and time into average speed (m/s or pace). Plot distance vs time; the slope is CS in m/s, and the y-intercept is D′ — the running analog of W′, measured in metres above CS that you can spend before being forced to slow down.

Swim — Critical Speed (CS swim)

What CS swim is. The pool analog of CP and run CS. The highest swim pace you can hold in quasi-steady state. Anchors every swim zone and every interval target.

The gold-standard protocol — 100 / 300 / 600. Three maximal time trials in the pool:

  • 100 m, maximal — the shortest trial, heavily reliant on anaerobic capacity; provides the VO2-speed proxy for swimming.
  • 300 m, maximal — severe domain; meaningful contribution from both aerobic and anaerobic systems.
  • 600 m, maximal — the longest and most durable trial. For a trained triathlete this takes roughly 8-12 minutes and sits close to CS itself.

Execution. 25 m or 50 m pool. Push-off starts only — no diving — so that the start is repeatable across retests. Each effort truly maximal. Warm-up: 800-1200 m of progressive swimming. Separate days, or full recovery if same-day.

Acceptable alternatives. If the 100 / 300 / 600 shape doesn't work for your pool access, ECHO also documents 200 / 400 / 800 m and 150 / 300 / 600 m as acceptable substitutes. Pacing discipline matters more than which distances you pick — the curve fit only works if each trial is truly maximal and evenly paced.

Heart rate — LTHR

What LTHR is. The HR ceiling at the top of Z1 — the heart rate you can sustain for roughly an hour of all-out steady effort. It's not your max HR. LTHR anchors the HR zones across all three sports.

The preferred field method. Prescribe 40-60 minutes at Z1 power or pace and watch the HR settle. The observed steady-state HR defines the top of Z1, and the HR zones are derived from that anchor as percentages of LTHR.

Fallbacks.

  • 30-minute all-out time trial — average HR of the final 20 minutes equals LTHR. Works when an extended Z1 session isn't practical.
  • Race-derived — for athletes with recent honest race files: the average HR from a steady, well-paced Ironman bike + 2 bpm is a reasonable LTHR proxy when no clean test is available.

Retest cadence — and when NOT to retest

Anchors update every 6-8 weeks, or when formal testing data is collected. Small, frequent adjustments are preferred over infrequent large corrections. Stale anchors cause every downstream zone, pacing target, and block decision to drift.

Do NOT update an anchor:

  • After a single great workout. One exceptional day does not establish a new anchor.
  • During heavy fatigue or a high-load phase. Fatigue masks fitness; testing fatigued produces values that underestimate true working capacity.
  • During illness, major life stress, or significant travel disruption.
  • To reward fitness or make the athlete feel good. Anchors reflect current working fitness, not potential.

Defending this distinction daily is one of the non-negotiable standards of ECHO. The number on the page has to match what the athlete can actually do today, not what they hope it will be next month.

The short version

  • Bike: CP from 3 / 6 / 12 minute maximal efforts. Three points so bad trials show up.
  • Run: CS from 3K + 10K (practical) or 400 / 800 / 3200 m (precise).
  • Swim: CS from 100 / 300 / 600 m push-off maximal trials.
  • HR: LTHR from 40-60 min of Z1 power/pace (preferred), 30-min all-out (fallback), or IM-bike-avg + 2 bpm (race-derived).
  • Cadence: retest every 6-8 weeks. Never on a great day, a tired day, a sick day, or to make the athlete feel good.

ECHO covers the math, the curve-fitting, the standard-error-of-estimate cross-check, and the W′ / D′ application. Foundations gives you the protocols and the rules so the anchors are honest before any of that decision logic gets layered on top.

Block Types

A block is a stretch of training (usually 1-6 weeks) with a single, focused purpose. Blocks are how a season gets built — you don't randomly mix every kind of work every week; you spend 3-6 weeks deepening one capacity, then transition to the next. That structure is what lets adaptation actually happen.

ECHO uses five foundational block types. Foundations introduces them at the vocabulary level — what each block is for, what it looks like, and roughly how long it lasts. The full ECHO Manual covers when to choose which block, how to sequence them across a year, and how to read the athlete's response to know when to transition.

Canonical reference (live from ECHO):
  • Base — Build aerobic capacity and durability. Mostly ZR-Z1 volume with steady Z2 introduction. Typical duration: 3-6 weeks.
  • Build — Introduce threshold and tempo work on top of aerobic base. Z2-Z3 emphasis. Typical duration: 3-4 weeks.
  • Peak — Race-specific intensity and durability. Mix of Z2-Z3 with race-pace work and selected VO2 Max touches. Typical duration: 2-3 weeks.
  • Taper — Reduce volume while preserving sharpness ahead of A-race. Typical duration: 1-2 weeks.
  • Recovery — Active recovery week, ~50-65% normal volume, ZR-Z1 only. Typical duration: 1 week.

Base — build the engine

Base is where the aerobic foundation and durability chassis are built. The work is mostly ZR and Z1 with steady Z2 introduction — long, low-cost, repeatable. This is the block most athletes want to skip and the block coaches most need to defend. There is no shortcut around it. An athlete with a thin Base never builds a high ceiling, because the floor under the ceiling isn't strong enough to support it.

What to expect: easy, conversational sessions; bike rides in the 2-6+ hour range as fitness develops; runs that feel almost too easy; gradual durability gains that won't show up on a stopwatch for several weeks. The point isn't to feel hard — the point is to feel repeatable.

Build — add the work

Build introduces real threshold and tempo work on top of the aerobic base — Z2 and Z3 efforts, structured intervals, race-adjacent sustained pieces. The character of the week shifts: there are still aerobic-volume sessions, but two or three sessions per week now carry meaningful intensity.

Build only works if Base is solid underneath it. If the athlete arrives at Build with a weak foundation, the harder sessions break them down instead of building them up. ECHO's read on this is consistent: when an athlete fades late in races or stalls in training, the answer is almost never "more Build" — it's "more Base."

Peak — sharpen and rehearse

Peak is the most race-specific block. It mixes Z2-Z3 race-pace work with selected VO2 Max touches and, for long-course athletes, race-rehearsal sessions that simulate the actual event demand. Volume usually backs off slightly from Build; intensity stays high but in tighter, more deliberate doses.

The goal in Peak is not new fitness — it's expressing the fitness already built. The athlete should feel sharp at the end of Peak, not exhausted. If they feel exhausted, Peak was too long, too dense, or built on a foundation that hadn't fully consolidated.

Taper — strip volume, hold sharpness

Taper is the 1-2 weeks before an A-race. Volume drops significantly; frequency stays familiar; selected race-pace touches stay in to keep the engine sharp. The aim is to arrive at the start line fresh, not tired — but still moving in race rhythm.

Taper feels weird. Athletes often report feeling flat, sluggish, or anxious in the final week. Taper sensations are not evidence of fitness loss. Trust the work; protect the sleep; keep the routine simple.

Recovery — the block that buys all the others

Recovery is an active recovery week, not a week off. Volume drops to roughly 50-65% of normal. Intensity stays in ZR-Z1 only — no threshold, no tempo, no intervals. Frequency may stay similar to a normal week, but every session is short, easy, and protective.

Recovery weeks are scheduled deliberately into the training year — typically every 3-4 weeks of harder work — because adaptation happens during recovery, not during the hard work itself. Athletes who skip recovery weeks don't get fitter faster; they accumulate fatigue until something breaks. The Recovery block is the one that makes every other block productive.

ECHO covers block selection — which block to pick for which athlete at which moment — using the full decision framework. Foundations gives you the vocabulary so you understand what your coach is choosing and why.

Fueling & Hydration

Fueling is not separate from training. It is training. Every session is the work plus the fueling that supported it — and a session executed without honest fueling is a different session than the one on the page. You can do everything else right and still finish a session, a race, or a season worse off because the fueling was wrong.

This section gives you three things: the simple framework we use to think about fueling on every session and every race (the Power of 3), the practical way to find your individual sweat losses (the sweat test), and an introduction to carbohydrate — the fuel that does most of the work in endurance sport.

The Power of 3

The Power of 3 is the simplest way to think about what you need during endurance work. Three things, in this order:

  1. Fluid — water you put back in to replace what you sweat out.
  2. Sodium — the primary electrolyte you lose in sweat. Sodium keeps fluid usable; without it, drinking more water actually makes you worse.
  3. Calories (carbohydrate) — the fuel your working muscles and your brain run on.

The reason fluid sits first is simple: an athlete who doesn't drink can't process sodium or carbohydrate properly. The reason sodium is second is just as practical: drinking large volumes of plain water without sodium dilutes your blood sodium, which makes you feel worse, perform worse, and in extreme cases puts you in real medical trouble. Carbohydrate is third because it does the heaviest lifting metabolically — but only if the fluid and sodium underneath it are in place.

This is the order to think in. It's also the order to fix problems in. If a session is going sideways, audit fluid first, sodium second, calories third. Most fueling problems are fluid problems pretending to be something else.

Hydration — finding YOUR sweat rate

Generic hydration guidance ("drink 500 ml per hour") is wrong for almost everyone. Sweat rates vary enormously — between athletes, between hot and cool days, between hard and easy sessions, between cycling and running. The only honest answer is to measure yours.

The sweat test — how to do it

  1. Empty your bladder before the session.
  2. Weigh yourself naked on a digital scale. Record the number.
  3. Train for exactly one hour at a typical session intensity. Track exactly how much fluid you drink during the hour, in ml or ounces.
  4. Towel off all sweat when you finish.
  5. Weigh yourself naked again. Record the number.

The math

Sweat rate per hour = (starting weight − ending weight) + fluid consumed

Convert weight loss to volume: 1 kg of body-weight loss ≈ 1 litre of sweat (or 1 lb ≈ 16 fl oz). Add the fluid you drank during the hour. That total is your sweat rate per hour for that intensity and that day.

Worked example. You start at 70.0 kg. You ride for one hour and drink 600 ml. You finish at 69.2 kg. Weight lost = 0.8 kg = 800 ml of sweat. Plus 600 ml drunk = 1,400 ml of sweat in one hour. That's your sweat rate for that session. On a hot day or a harder session, it will be higher; on a cool day or an easy session, lower.

What to do with the number

The goal during a session is to replace most of what you lose — typically 60-80% of your sweat rate, not 100%. The gut can only absorb so much fluid per hour, and chasing 100% replacement usually causes sloshing, bloating, or worse. Aim to finish a normal training session no more than 2% lighter than you started; finish a race in the same window.

Retest seasonally. Sweat rate changes with heat acclimation, fitness, and time of year. Do a sweat test in early summer when you start hitting heat, again mid-season, and once when the cold weather settles in. You don't need it every week — but the once-a-year number is rarely the right one for the day you're working in.

Sodium

Sodium is what makes the fluid you drink usable. It pulls water into your bloodstream and out of your gut, helps maintain blood volume, and keeps your muscles and nerves firing normally. Sweat is salty — every litre carries somewhere between roughly 400 mg and 1,500+ mg of sodium depending on the athlete. Heavy sweaters and "salty sweaters" (you'll see white crust on dark training kit) sit at the high end.

A general starting range during endurance work is 500-1,000 mg of sodium per hour, scaled up in heat, up for heavy/salty sweaters, and up further for long-course racing. Most commercial sports drinks sit at the low end of that range; long-course athletes typically need to supplement with electrolyte tablets, salt capsules, or higher-concentration mixes.

The two failure modes:

  • Too little sodium — heavy fatigue late in long sessions, cramping, nausea, brain fog, sometimes a swollen feeling in the hands or face from drinking water without enough salt. Hyponatremia at the extreme.
  • Too much sodium without fluid — stomach upset, salty taste, eventually nausea. Rarely the actual problem in endurance sport unless you're dosing salt without drinking.

Sodium is the variable most athletes under-do. If a session or race went sideways and the fluid was right, look at sodium next.

Carbohydrate — the engine fuel

Carbohydrate is the primary fuel for endurance work above easy aerobic intensities. Your body stores it as glycogen in the muscles and liver — a finite reserve, typically enough for 90-120 minutes of moderate work before it runs low. Fat is the other major fuel, and at low intensities you can burn fat almost indefinitely. As intensity rises, the mix shifts hard toward carbohydrate. Above threshold, you're running almost entirely on carb.

This is why long-course racing lives or dies on carbohydrate strategy. The moment you run out of glycogen — "bonking," "hitting the wall," "going hypo" — the pace you can sustain drops dramatically, the brain gets foggy, and the day usually ends in damage-control mode. Good carb fueling extends how long you can hold race pace before that wall arrives.

Hourly carbohydrate ranges

The right number depends on how long you're going and how hard. General starting ranges:

  • Sessions under 60 min — generally no carbohydrate needed during the session. Water and sodium only. Eat before, refuel after.
  • 60-120 min, moderate intensity — roughly 30-60 g of carbohydrate per hour.
  • 2-4 hour sessions / Olympic + half-distance racing — roughly 60-90 g per hour.
  • 4+ hour sessions / Ironman racing — roughly 90-120 g per hour, sometimes higher for trained guts.

Those upper-end numbers (90+ g/hr) require a trained gut. The gut adapts to high-volume carbohydrate intake the same way muscles adapt to training — gradually, with repetition. An athlete who has never taken in more than 40 g/hr cannot suddenly hit 100 g/hr on race day without gut distress. Build the intake in training, the same way you build the fitness.

Carb sources — what counts as a gram

Read labels, not branding. A gram of carb is a gram of carb, whether it comes from a gel, a drink mix, a chew, a banana, a rice cake, or a sports bar. What matters is:

  • Total grams per hour — measured, not guessed.
  • Carbohydrate type — multiple-transport carbs (glucose + fructose blends, typically in roughly a 2:1 ratio) absorb at higher rates than glucose alone. Most modern sports nutrition products are built around this.
  • Form — a mix of liquid, semi-solid (gels, chews), and occasionally real food tends to sit better in the gut than four hours of one format only.

The two non-negotiables

  1. Rehearse in training. Race day is not the day to try a new product, a new flavor, or a new intake rate. Long sessions are the rehearsal — the gut, the timing, the flavor fatigue, the bottle layout, all of it.
  2. Plan grams first, products second. Decide how many grams per hour you need, then assemble the products that get you there. Most athletes who fade late didn't have a math problem — they had a plan problem.

The athlete-facing summary

  • Fluid first, sodium second, calories third — the Power of 3.
  • Know your sweat rate. Do a sweat test seasonally. Replace 60-80% during the session.
  • Sodium 500-1,000 mg/hr as a starting range. Up for heat, up for heavy/salty sweaters, up for long course.
  • Carb scales with duration: none under 60 min · 30-60 g/hr for 1-2 hr · 60-90 g/hr for 2-4 hr · 90-120+ g/hr for 4+ hr (trained gut).
  • Train the gut. Rehearse every product and every rate in training, not on race day.
  • Plan grams, not products. Decide the math first, fit the products to it.

Fueling is one of the most individual parts of endurance sport. The numbers above are starting ranges — your actual targets will emerge from sweat tests, training rehearsals, and the feedback you give your coach session by session. Bring fueling notes to every review.

Mental Foundations

Training load, fueling, and recovery are the physical pillars of performance. Mental skill is what determines whether all of that preparation actually shows up under pressure — in the long session, the bad day, the race that's not going to plan, the comeback after an injury, the third year of training when novelty stops doing the motivational work for you.

Mental skills are skills. They're trained, not innate. The same way you don't get a stronger aerobic engine by hoping for one, you don't get a tougher mind by hoping for one. This section covers the foundational concepts every athlete in the EchoDevo system should know — at the introductory level. The deeper work (Mindset Types, archetypes, athlete-specific coaching responses) lives in the full ECHO framework. Here, we cover the universal foundations.

Self-Efficacy — the most important mental variable

Self-efficacy is the belief that you can do the thing in front of you. Not the belief that you're talented; not the belief that you deserve to succeed; not motivation, which comes and goes. Self-efficacy is the calm, working-memory-level conviction that the task is doable and that you are the person who can do it. It's the most reliable single predictor of athletic performance once basic fitness is in place.

Self-efficacy is built deliberately:

  • Mastery experiences — actually doing hard things and surviving them. Every long session you finish, every hard interval set you complete, every race you execute is a deposit. There is no substitute for this.
  • Vicarious experiences — watching athletes you identify with succeed under similar conditions. Training partners, teammates, your coach's other athletes. "If they can do it, I can do it."
  • Verbal persuasion — a coach or trusted partner telling you clearly that you are capable. Less powerful than the first two, but real.
  • Physiological state — interpreting your body's signals (nerves, elevated HR, butterflies) as readiness rather than as threat. Same physiology, different story.

The practical move: collect mastery. Keep a record of the hard sessions you executed cleanly. When confidence wobbles, you read the record. It is much harder to talk yourself out of a thing you have already done.

Goals, Targets, and Outcomes

Three different things, often confused. Confusing them is one of the most common reasons athletes lose motivation, peak too early, or crash after a race.

  • Outcomes are results you don't fully control. "Win my age group." "Qualify for Worlds." "Sub-3 marathon." Outcomes depend on weather, competition, race-day execution, and luck. They're motivating to point at, but they're a bad place to put your daily attention.
  • Targets are performance benchmarks you mostly control. "Run 1:25 for the half." "Hold 250 W average on the bike split." "Swim 1:35/100 m for the open water leg." Targets are what we train toward. They're concrete, measurable, and largely within your control on a good day.
  • Goals — or process goals — are the daily and weekly behaviors that make targets and outcomes possible. "Five swims a week for six weeks." "Don't miss the long ride." "Eat the prescribed grams every Saturday." "Sleep 8 hours on weeknights." Goals are entirely within your control. They are what you actually do.

The pyramid: Outcomes ride on Targets. Targets ride on Goals. Goals ride on daily behavior. When athletes get stuck or burned out, it's almost always because they've inverted the pyramid — staring at outcomes every day instead of executing goals every day. Coach yourself the right way up.

Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic Motivation

Intrinsic motivation is doing the work because the work itself matters to you. You like training. You like the long ride. You like getting better. The activity is its own reward.

Extrinsic motivation is doing the work for an external payoff — a medal, a podium, a Kona slot, social recognition, a coach's approval. Real, useful, sometimes powerful — but fragile. Extrinsic motivation evaporates the day the external reward stops feeling worth the cost.

Both kinds of motivation drive performance. The athletes who last for years are the ones whose core motivation is intrinsic, with extrinsic motivation layered on top as accelerant rather than as fuel. If the only reason you train is the medal, the season you don't get one becomes the season you quit.

Practical move: every few months, ask yourself why you're doing this. If the only honest answer is the result, fold a piece of process back into the answer. Find a part of the training itself that you like. Identify a non-result way the sport is making you a better human. Keep the intrinsic engine running.

Growth Mindset

A fixed mindset believes ability is mostly innate — you have it or you don't. A growth mindset believes ability is developed through effort, strategy, and learning over time. Both mindsets are present in every athlete; the question is which one is louder when the work gets hard.

The signs of a growth mindset showing up under stress:

  • Treats setbacks as information, not as verdicts.
  • Asks "what did this teach me?" before "what does this mean about me?"
  • Sees the harder version of the workout as the more interesting one.
  • Doesn't compare today's effort to a peak day; compares it to what's possible today.
  • Talks about progress in terms of process, not in terms of inherent talent.

The signs of a fixed mindset:

  • "I'm just not a fast runner." "I've never been good at swimming." "I don't have the genetics for this."
  • Avoiding sessions where the athlete might look bad.
  • Treating one bad workout as evidence about identity, rather than as data about a day.
  • Comparing self to others as a verdict on worth rather than as information about training.

You will catch yourself in fixed-mindset thinking. Everyone does. The skill is noticing it, naming it, and reframing the same situation in growth-mindset terms. With practice, the growth frame becomes the default — but it has to be practiced like any other skill.

Daily Reflection

The most underused tool in endurance sport is a 5-minute end-of-day note. Three questions, every day:

  1. What went well today? (Training, fueling, sleep, mood, anything.)
  2. What got in the way? (Sessions skipped, fueling missed, stress that bled into the session, anything.)
  3. What is the one thing I will do differently tomorrow? (Concrete. Singular. Doable.)

Don't write a journal. Write three answers. Two minutes. The reason it works isn't catharsis — it's pattern detection. After two weeks you start seeing what actually correlates with good sessions and bad ones. After two months you stop being surprised by your own performance, because you can see the inputs.

Bring the reflection notes to coach check-ins. They give your coach signal that no power file can provide.

Gratitude

Endurance sport is voluntary. It is also, by definition, hard. Every athlete eventually hits a stretch where the volume feels like a burden, the alarm clock feels like an enemy, and the long session ahead feels like a punishment. That is the moment gratitude becomes a performance tool, not a self-help cliché.

Gratitude is the deliberate practice of noticing what's working. Not pretending hard things aren't hard — naming the things that are still good while the hard things are also true. "This is a hard session AND I'm lucky to have a body that can do this." Both can be true. Holding both makes the session executable.

A simple practice: at the start of every long session, name one thing — out loud or in your head — that you are grateful for in this moment. The body. The weather. A training partner. A coach. The sport itself. It takes ten seconds. It changes the next four hours.

Comparison

Comparison is the most reliable way to make yourself miserable in endurance sport. There will always be someone faster, stronger, leaner, more talented, more decorated, more well-funded, more available. Social media has made this problem ten times worse — every athlete now has an audience they didn't choose, watching highlights of strangers they don't know.

The hard truth: comparison to other athletes tells you almost nothing useful. It tells you nothing about your physiology, your training history, your life context, your response to load. It only tells you that someone else, with completely different inputs, produced a different output.

The only comparison that produces signal is comparison to your own past self. Are you stronger than you were six months ago? Are you fueling better than you were a year ago? Is your durability higher than it was last season? Those are answerable, informative, and within your control.

Practical move: when you notice yourself comparing to other athletes, ask one question — "Does this person's training history, life, and physiology match mine?" The honest answer is always no. Then put your attention back on your own data and your own next session.

The mental foundations summary

  • Self-efficacy is the single most important mental variable. Build it through mastery — by actually doing hard things and keeping a record.
  • Goals, Targets, Outcomes — coach the pyramid right-side up. Daily behavior > targets > outcomes.
  • Intrinsic before extrinsic. Find what about the sport itself you love. Layer external rewards on top, not underneath.
  • Growth mindset — setbacks are information, not verdicts. Catch the fixed frame and reframe it.
  • Daily reflection — three questions, two minutes, every day. Pattern detection beats memory.
  • Gratitude — name what's good while the hard things are also true.
  • Comparison — only useful against your own past self. Other athletes are not the comparison set.

Mental skills are trained over years, not weeks. Bring what's working and what's not to every coach check-in. These foundations carry forward into every block, every race, and every season that follows.
