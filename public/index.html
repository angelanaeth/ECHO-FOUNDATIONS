The Two Foundational Rules

Before any of the technical content matters, two principles sit underneath all of it.

1. Personalization — Train YOUR body, not a generic plan.
Every athlete has unique physiology, life context, training history, and response to load. A workout that builds one athlete will break another. The numbers we set — heart rate zones, power zones, paces, fueling targets — are derived from that specific athlete's testing and history, not from a chart in a magazine.

2. Use data and principles to make decisions.
Coaching is not guessing. We anchor every training and pacing decision in objective signals: Critical Power, Critical Speed, heart rate response, perceived exertion, and how the athlete is actually adapting week to week. Vibes are not a plan.

These two rules pull in opposite directions on purpose. Personalization keeps us from rigid template-coaching. Data keeps us from drifting into "whatever feels right." Holding both at the same time is what separates coaching from cheerleading.

Zones

Zones are the language of training. They turn a vague instruction like "go easy" or "push it" into something specific, measurable, and repeatable. Every workout in your plan will be prescribed in zones — so the goal of this section is simple: by the end of it, you should know what each zone means, how to find it, and how to know you're in it.

ECHO uses one shared 5-band system across every modality: ZR · Z1 · Z2 · Z3 · VO2 Max. The bands stay the same whether you're talking about heart rate, bike power, run pace, or swim pace — what changes is the anchor each modality is built from. This shared vocabulary is intentional: when your coach says "Z2 ride" or "Z3 run repeats," you should be able to hold those two ideas in the same shape in your head.

The four ways to measure effort

  • Heart rate (HR) — internal cost. Universal. Slightly delayed and influenced by heat, sleep, hydration, and stress.
  • Power (bike) — external output, instantaneous, no lag. The cleanest bike anchor when available.
  • Pace (run, swim) — external output expressed as time-per-distance. The cleanest run and swim anchor.
  • RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion) — subjective effort. Not a primary zone system in ECHO — it's the verification and safety-valve. If the output target says 250W but RPE says 10/10 on a Z2 day, the day is wrong, not the math.

You don't need all four to train well. The rule we work from is Anchor → Range → Verify: test the output anchor (CP for bike, CS for run and swim), build the zone ranges from that anchor, and verify internal cost with HR, breathing, and RPE. Then apply context — heat, terrain, fatigue, fueling — on top.

The bands (what they're for)

  • ZR — Recovery. Active regeneration, reset work, low-cost movement. Unlimited duration. If you finish a ZR session feeling worked, it wasn't ZR.
  • Z1 — Durability Base. Primary low-cost volume; chassis-building. Easy steady efforts in the 2-6+ hour range on the bike. This is where the aerobic engine and the durability foundation are built. The majority of your training year lives in ZR-Z1.
  • Z2 — Steady / Durable. Race-pace feel for long-course racing. Sustained aerobic work, race-adjacent control, 1-3 hours on the bike. Sub-threshold but real work — talking shrinks to short phrases.
  • Z3 — Threshold / Critical. Hard sustained efforts at or near threshold. 20-90 minutes total of work, usually delivered as intervals. This is where you train sustainable expression directly — the ceiling of "sustainable hard."
  • VO2 Max — Peak / Ceiling. Short maximal-aerobic intervals, typically 3-8 minutes. Builds the ceiling, not the floor. High cost, low repeatability, and only useful when the structure underneath it exists.

HR Zones — % of LTHR

LTHR is your Lactate Threshold Heart Rate: the heart rate you can hold for roughly one hour of all-out effort. It's not your max HR. Once we know your LTHR, every HR zone is a percentage of that number. (How we test for LTHR lives in Section 3.)

Canonical reference (live from ECHO): these values are auto-synced. Do not edit by hand.
  • ZR — Recovery — 65-76% of LTHR
  • Deliberate gap: 76-80% LTHR — see callout below
  • Z1 — Durability Base — 80-86% of LTHR
  • Z2 — Steady / Durable — 86-93% of LTHR
  • Z3 — Threshold / Critical — 93-100% of LTHR
  • VO2 Max — HR is a lagging confirmation only. HR cannot reliably govern VO2 work because it ramps too slowly to catch short intervals. Use power or pace, with RPE as the cross-check.
The deliberate ZR ↔ Z1 HR gap (76-80% LTHR)

HR is the one zone family where ZR and Z1 are not adjacent. ZR tops out at 76% LTHR. Z1 starts at 80% LTHR. The 4-point window from 76-80% is intentional — it is the "neither" zone where the athlete is neither truly recovering nor producing genuine durability-base stimulus.

How to read it:

  • Recovery day, HR drifting into 76-80%? The session is no longer recovery. Pull intensity back to true ZR (slow your jog, soft-pedal more) or stop the workout. Recovery that costs Z1 internal load isn't recovery — it's undertraining and under-recovery at the same time.
  • Z1 long ride/run, HR sitting in 76-80% instead of 80-86%? Two possibilities: (a) the session is under-stimulating — the chassis-building work isn't happening, push a touch firmer; or (b) HR is suppressed by accumulated fatigue, under-fueling, illness brewing, or environment (cold, altitude). Investigate before you push.

The bike-power, run-pace, and swim-pace bands are still adjacent — power and pace are external outputs governed by physics. HR is the internal-cost verifier, and the 76-80% dead-zone forces honest answers about which side of the recovery/base line the athlete is actually on.

Bike Power Zones — % of CP

CP (Critical Power) is the highest power you can sustain in a quasi-steady state — the horizontal asymptote of your power-duration curve. CP is the bike anchor for every zone, every interval target, and every pacing decision. Section 3 covers the 3 / 6 / 12-minute test we use to find it.

Canonical reference (live from ECHO):
  • ZR — Recovery — < 65% of CP · unlimited duration
  • Z1 — Durability Base — 65-79% of CP · 2-6+ hour efforts
  • Z2 — Steady / Durable — 79-89% of CP · 1-3 hours
  • Z3 — Threshold / Critical — 89-100% of CP · 20-90 min
  • VO2 Max — 100-120% of CP · 3-8 min intervals

Power is instantaneous — it tells you what you're doing right now, with no lag. HR tells you what your body is responding to, which is delayed by 30-60 seconds. Use both. When power and HR disagree, treat it as information — not a problem to solve. Heat, dehydration, poor sleep, or accumulated fatigue all show up as HR running high (or sometimes low) for a given power output. The right move depends on which signal the day belongs to.

Run Zones — % of CS

CS (Critical Speed) is the running analog of CP — the highest pace you can sustain in a quasi-steady state. CS is the run anchor, and pace is the cleanest field expression of sustainable run performance. Run zones are built from CS, and HR is used to verify the cost.

Canonical reference (live from ECHO):
  • ZR — Recovery — slower than 75% of CS pace · HR 65-76% LTHR · regeneration and reset
  • Z1 — Durability Base — 75-85% of CS pace · HR 80-86% LTHR (gap from 76-80%) · primary low-cost run volume
  • Z2 — Steady / Durable — 85-95% of CS pace · HR 86-93% LTHR · steady, durability support, race-adjacent
  • Z3 — Threshold / Critical — 95-105% of CS pace · HR 93-100% LTHR · threshold / critical-speed work
  • VO2 Max — 105-120% of CS pace · HR lagging confirmation only · 3-8 min interval work

Reminder: run pace is time-per-distance (e.g. 4:30/km), so higher % of CS pace = slower pace. Only VO2 Max is faster than CS itself.

Run power. If you train with a run power meter, run power should mirror CS, not replace it. CS is the field-tested reality; watts help you execute that reality on hills and uneven terrain. When watts and CS disagree, trust the field-tested CS anchor first — then use watts as a secondary execution aid. On hot days, hills, or off-road, HR and RPE will be more honest than pace.

Swim Zones — % of CS (swim)

Swim CS is the pool analog of CP and run CS — the highest swim pace you can hold in a quasi-steady state. Testing protocols (multiple distances, e.g. 200 / 400 / 800 or 200 / 400 / 1500) live in Section 3.

Swim zones compress around CS more tightly than bike or run because of how sensitive swimming is to technique. The percentage bands look small — that's correct.

Canonical reference (live from ECHO):
  • ZR — Recovery — 90-95% of CS · unlimited duration · easy technique work
  • Z1 — Durability Base — 95-97% of CS · 45-90 min · long aerobic swim volume
  • Z2 — Steady / Durable — 97-99% of CS · 30-60 min · sustained aerobic, race-pace for long course
  • Z3 — Threshold / Critical — 99-102% of CS · 20-40 min · threshold / critical-speed swim work
  • Above CS — intervals only:
    • Repeatable: CS + 5% — e.g. 6-8 × 200m
    • Short max: CS + 10% — e.g. 4-8 × 25-100m

Hold the Water

Swim intensity is governed by technique before it is governed by effort. You can muscle through a hard bike or run set — you cannot muscle through swim intensity without losing the catch, the rhythm, and the energy economy that make swimming work.

The phrase we use is Hold the Water. Feel the pressure on your forearm and palm through the entire pull, on every stroke. If a set forces you to lose your hold, the prescribed intensity was too high for that day — slow down and keep the technique intact. Speed in the water comes from a longer, cleaner stroke, not a more violent one.

This is why swim zones compress around CS the way they do, and why above-CS work is delivered as discrete intervals rather than sustained efforts. Hold the water first, then add pressure.

RPE — the verification and safety valve

RPE is not a primary zone system in ECHO. It's the verification layer: a way to check whether the prescribed output is actually affordable for the athlete on the day.

The rule: RPE outranks the anchor when internal strain spikes. If the output target says 250W and the athlete reports RPE 10/10 on what should be a Z2 day, the day is wrong, not the math. Pull back the output, finish the session at an honest cost, and figure out the why afterward (heat? illness? sleep debt? fueling? life stress?).

That's the safety-valve job RPE does. Every athlete should be in the habit of rating sessions in plain language — "easy," "steady," "hard," "very hard," "maxed out" — and reporting it. The number itself matters less than the trend over time relative to the prescribed work.

How they fit together — Anchor → Range → Verify

  • 1. Anchor — test the output anchor (CP for bike, CS for run and swim, LTHR for HR). Use the best anchor available; fallbacks only when a real test isn't possible.
  • 2. Range — build ZR / Z1 / Z2 / Z3 / VO2 Max from the anchor. Do not blur the edges to match athlete preference.
  • 3. Verify — check internal cost with HR drift, breathing, and RPE. If the verification doesn't match the anchor, that's information.
  • 4. Apply context — heat, terrain, fatigue, and fueling can override. Zones are anchors, not permission to ignore reality.

Testing — CP, CS, and LTHR

Zones only mean something if the anchor underneath them is real. If CP, CS, or LTHR is wrong, every zone is wrong — and every block decision built on those zones drifts with them. That's why testing is its own section, not a footnote.

The goal of this section is to give you the three field protocols ECHO actually uses, the rules for when to retest, and the rules for when not to retest.

The principle — Anchor first

ECHO is built on three field-tested anchors: CP for the bike, CS for the run, CS (swim) for the pool, and LTHR as the universal HR anchor. Every zone, every interval target, every race-pace number, and every block decision in ECHO flows downstream from those four numbers.

If they are wrong, every downstream prescription is wrong. If they are stale, the system drifts. If they are defended — kept current on a sensible cadence, never inflated to flatter the athlete — the coaching decisions on top of them become defensible, repeatable, and productive.

Bike — Critical Power (CP)

What CP is. The horizontal asymptote of your power-duration curve — the highest power you can sustain in a quasi-steady state. CP is not FTP. FTP is a single 60-minute estimate; CP comes from multiple maximal efforts that expose how your output actually changes with duration. CP is also why we use three test points and not two: with three points, a poorly paced trial shows up as error in the fit. With two points, every line looks perfect — there is no way to detect a bad trial.

The 3 / 6 / 12 protocol. Three maximal efforts on the bike:

  • 3-minute maximal effort — the shortest and most violent. The athlete rides as hard as possible for the full three minutes. The average power from this effort also serves as a proxy for the power at VO2 max.
  • 6-minute maximal effort — sits in the upper severe domain. Draws heavily on both aerobic and anaerobic capacity; provides the second point on the curve.
  • 12-minute maximal effort — the longest trial, sitting closest to CP itself. Anchors the lower end of the curve and is the most durable of the three.

Execution. Tests can be performed on separate days, or on the same day in descending order of duration (12 → 6 → 3) with 30 minutes of active recovery between efforts. Each effort must be truly maximal — the athlete should finish knowing that continuing for even 10 more seconds at that power would not have been possible. A thorough warm-up of 15-20 minutes including progressive builds and one short hard opener is required before the first test of any session.

What you do with the numbers. Plot total work (W = power × time, in joules) against time. Three points. The slope of the linear regression is CP. The y-intercept is W′ (the finite reserve above CP). The math can be done in a spreadsheet, in WKO5, Golden Cheetah, or intervals.icu — Foundations doesn't require you to do it by hand. ECHO covers the deeper interpretation.

FTP fallback. When a clean three-point test isn't available, the standard FTP fallbacks (0.95 of a 20-min effort, or 0.9 of the average of two 8-min efforts) can be used. Treat the result as approximate, not as a substitute for CP.

Run — Critical Speed (CS)

What CS is. The running analog of CP — the highest pace you can sustain in a quasi-steady state. CS is the asymptote of your pace-vs-duration curve. Run zones are built from CS, and run power (if you train with it) is anchored back to CS rather than guessed from a device default.

The protocol. Multi-distance maximal time trials on flat, measured ground. ECHO documents two acceptable shapes:

  • 3K + 10K time trials on flat terrain — the practical option for most athletes. Two well-paced maximal efforts on separate days.
  • 400 / 800 / 3200 m on a track for tighter curve-fitting. The 400 m sits high on the curve (proxy for sVO2 max), the 800 m sits in the severe domain, and the 3200 m anchors the bottom of the curve near CS itself.

Execution. Track preferred for the 400/800/3200 version. Each effort must be truly maximal. Warm up with 15-20 minutes of easy running followed by 3-4 strides. If running multiple trials on the same day, give substantial recovery between them; otherwise separate days are cleaner.

What you do with the numbers. Convert each distance and time into average speed (m/s or pace). Plot distance vs time; the slope is CS in m/s, and the y-intercept is D′ — the running analog of W′, measured in metres above CS that you can spend before being forced to slow down.

Swim — Critical Speed (CS swim)

What CS swim is. The pool analog of CP and run CS. The highest swim pace you can hold in quasi-steady state. Anchors every swim zone and every interval target.

The gold-standard protocol — 100 / 300 / 600. Three maximal time trials in the pool:

  • 100 m, maximal — the shortest trial, heavily reliant on anaerobic capacity; provides the VO2-speed proxy for swimming.
  • 300 m, maximal — severe domain; meaningful contribution from both aerobic and anaerobic systems.
  • 600 m, maximal — the longest and most durable trial. For a trained triathlete this takes roughly 8-12 minutes and sits close to CS itself.

Execution. 25 m or 50 m pool. Push-off starts only — no diving — so that the start is repeatable across retests. Each effort truly maximal. Warm-up: 800-1200 m of progressive swimming. Separate days, or full recovery if same-day.

Acceptable alternatives. If the 100 / 300 / 600 shape doesn't work for your pool access, ECHO also documents 200 / 400 / 800 m and 150 / 300 / 600 m as acceptable substitutes. Pacing discipline matters more than which distances you pick — the curve fit only works if each trial is truly maximal and evenly paced.

Heart rate — LTHR

What LTHR is. The HR ceiling at the top of Z1 — the heart rate you can sustain for roughly an hour of all-out steady effort. It's not your max HR. LTHR anchors the HR zones across all three sports.

The preferred field method. Prescribe 40-60 minutes at Z1 power or pace and watch the HR settle. The observed steady-state HR defines the top of Z1, and the HR zones are derived from that anchor as percentages of LTHR.

Fallbacks.

  • 30-minute all-out time trial — average HR of the final 20 minutes equals LTHR. Works when an extended Z1 session isn't practical.
  • Race-derived — for athletes with recent honest race files: the average HR from a steady, well-paced Ironman bike + 2 bpm is a reasonable LTHR proxy when no clean test is available.

Retest cadence — and when NOT to retest

Anchors update every 6-8 weeks, or when formal testing data is collected. Small, frequent adjustments are preferred over infrequent large corrections. Stale anchors cause every downstream zone, pacing target, and block decision to drift.

Do NOT update an anchor:

  • After a single great workout. One exceptional day does not establish a new anchor.
  • During heavy fatigue or a high-load phase. Fatigue masks fitness; testing fatigued produces values that underestimate true working capacity.
  • During illness, major life stress, or significant travel disruption.
  • To reward fitness or make the athlete feel good. Anchors reflect current working fitness, not potential.

Defending this distinction daily is one of the non-negotiable standards of ECHO. The number on the page has to match what the athlete can actually do today, not what they hope it will be next month.

The short version

  • Bike: CP from 3 / 6 / 12 minute maximal efforts. Three points so bad trials show up.
  • Run: CS from 3K + 10K (practical) or 400 / 800 / 3200 m (precise).
  • Swim: CS from 100 / 300 / 600 m push-off maximal trials.
  • HR: LTHR from 40-60 min of Z1 power/pace (preferred), 30-min all-out (fallback), or IM-bike-avg + 2 bpm (race-derived).
  • Cadence: retest every 6-8 weeks. Never on a great day, a tired day, a sick day, or to make the athlete feel good.

ECHO covers the math, the curve-fitting, the standard-error-of-estimate cross-check, and the W′ / D′ application. Foundations gives you the protocols and the rules so the anchors are honest before any of that decision logic gets layered on top.

Block Types

A block is a stretch of training (usually 1-6 weeks) with a single, focused purpose. Blocks are how a season gets built — you don't randomly mix every kind of work every week; you spend 3-6 weeks deepening one capacity, then transition to the next. That structure is what lets adaptation actually happen.

ECHO uses five foundational block types. Foundations introduces them at the vocabulary level — what each block is for, what it looks like, and roughly how long it lasts. The full ECHO Manual covers when to choose which block, how to sequence them across a year, and how to read the athlete's response to know when to transition.

Canonical reference (live from ECHO):
  • Base — Build aerobic capacity and durability. Mostly ZR-Z1 volume with steady Z2 introduction. Typical duration: 3-6 weeks.
  • Build — Introduce threshold and tempo work on top of aerobic base. Z2-Z3 emphasis. Typical duration: 3-4 weeks.
  • Peak — Race-specific intensity and durability. Mix of Z2-Z3 with race-pace work and selected VO2 Max touches. Typical duration: 2-3 weeks.
  • Taper — Reduce volume while preserving sharpness ahead of A-race. Typical duration: 1-2 weeks.
  • Recovery — Active recovery week, ~50-65% normal volume, ZR-Z1 only. Typical duration: 1 week.

Base — build the engine

Base is where the aerobic foundation and durability chassis are built. The work is mostly ZR and Z1 with steady Z2 introduction — long, low-cost, repeatable. This is the block most athletes want to skip and the block coaches most need to defend. There is no shortcut around it. An athlete with a thin Base never builds a high ceiling, because the floor under the ceiling isn't strong enough to support it.

What to expect: easy, conversational sessions; bike rides in the 2-6+ hour range as fitness develops; runs that feel almost too easy; gradual durability gains that won't show up on a stopwatch for several weeks. The point isn't to feel hard — the point is to feel repeatable.

Build — add the work

Build introduces real threshold and tempo work on top of the aerobic base — Z2 and Z3 efforts, structured intervals, race-adjacent sustained pieces. The character of the week shifts: there are still aerobic-volume sessions, but two or three sessions per week now carry meaningful intensity.

Build only works if Base is solid underneath it. If the athlete arrives at Build with a weak foundation, the harder sessions break them down instead of building them up. ECHO's read on this is consistent: when an athlete fades late in races or stalls in training, the answer is almost never "more Build" — it's "more Base."

Peak — sharpen and rehearse

Peak is the most race-specific block. It mixes Z2-Z3 race-pace work with selected VO2 Max touches and, for long-course athletes, race-rehearsal sessions that simulate the actual event demand. Volume usually backs off slightly from Build; intensity stays high but in tighter, more deliberate doses.

The goal in Peak is not new fitness — it's expressing the fitness already built. The athlete should feel sharp at the end of Peak, not exhausted. If they feel exhausted, Peak was too long, too dense, or built on a foundation that hadn't fully consolidated.

Taper — strip volume, hold sharpness

Taper is the 1-2 weeks before an A-race. Volume drops significantly; frequency stays familiar; selected race-pace touches stay in to keep the engine sharp. The aim is to arrive at the start line fresh, not tired — but still moving in race rhythm.

Taper feels weird. Athletes often report feeling flat, sluggish, or anxious in the final week. Taper sensations are not evidence of fitness loss. Trust the work; protect the sleep; keep the routine simple.

Recovery — the block that buys all the others

Recovery is an active recovery week, not a week off. Volume drops to roughly 50-65% of normal. Intensity stays in ZR-Z1 only — no threshold, no tempo, no intervals. Frequency may stay similar to a normal week, but every session is short, easy, and protective.

Recovery weeks are scheduled deliberately into the training year — typically every 3-4 weeks of harder work — because adaptation happens during recovery, not during the hard work itself. Athletes who skip recovery weeks don't get fitter faster; they accumulate fatigue until something breaks. The Recovery block is the one that makes every other block productive.

ECHO covers block selection — which block to pick for which athlete at which moment — using the full decision framework. Foundations gives you the vocabulary so you understand what your coach is choosing and why.

Repeatable, Recoverable, Progressive — the three filters on every plan

Before any block goes on an athlete's calendar, it gets run through three filters. These three words are the cleanest way to describe whether a training plan is going to work for a specific human being, or whether it's going to break them in week 3 and they're going to disappear.

  1. Repeatable — can this week happen again next week, and the week after, given the athlete's actual life? If the answer is "only if every single thing goes perfectly," it's not repeatable. If the answer is "yes, even when work spikes or the kid gets sick," it is. Repeatable = realistic, not heroic.
  2. Recoverable — can the athlete absorb this week given their total stress load (training + work + family + sleep)? Adaptation happens during recovery, not during the work. If the week leaves them cooked — deep fatigue, can't sleep, ATL spike with no rebound — it's not recoverable. Drop it.
  3. Progressive — over the next 3-6 weeks, is the trajectory moving forward? Not every week, but in aggregate. Progression is slow — 5-10% per week, not 50% jumps. If the athlete is doing the same 10 hours for 12 weeks, that's stagnation. If they're spiking from 10 to 16 in one week, that's injury risk dressed up as ambition.

The most common coaching failure is writing a plan that satisfies one of these (usually "progressive") at the cost of the other two. The athlete shows up week one inspired, week three exhausted, week five gone. ECHO blocks are designed to satisfy all three, every time, for the human in front of you — not the idealized version of them.

Training Levels & the Four Anchors Weekly system

You have the zones. You have the block types. Now: how do you actually structure a week? Most age-group athletes — and a lot of newer coaches — get this part wrong. They follow a plan written for someone with a different life, they protect the wrong sessions when the week falls apart, and they end up doing 80% of the work for 40% of the adaptation.

This section gives you two coaching tools that go together: training levels (how much volume the athlete can absorb this specific week) and the four anchors (which four sessions get protected before anything else).

ECHO compliance check

All intensity references in this section use ECHO's zone vocabulary (ZR / Z1 / Z2 / Z3 / VO2 Max) anchored to CP, CS, swim CS, and LTHR. The level framework and anchor framework are scheduling tools — they answer "how many hours and which sessions," not "what zone." Zone selection always flows from the active block (Base, Build, Peak, Taper, or Recovery) per the ECHO Manual.

The three training levels — pick one per week

An athlete's training capacity is not fixed. It moves with life. A great coach doesn't write one plan and bludgeon the athlete with it for 12 weeks — they write a system that flexes to this week's reality. Three levels:

Level Volume / wk When to use What's in the week
Level 1
Survival		 6-8 h Life stress is high. Work deadline, family crisis, illness recovery, heavy travel, poor sleep stack. Anchors only — short long ride, short long run, one easy swim, optional easy spin. Zero intensity. All ZR-Z1. The goal is maintain base, not build.
Level 2
Solid work		 9-11 h Normal life, normal stress, recovery is working. This is the default. If you don't know which level, pick this one. All four anchors + 2-3 easy fill sessions. Distribution: 85-90% in ZR-Z1-Z2 (easy and steady), 10-15% in Z3 (one threshold session). Sustainable for months.
Level 3
Full training		 12-15 h Life stress is low, sleep is dialed, recovery markers are green. Peak weeks of the Build or Peak block when everything aligns. All anchors + optional doubles (short easy bike + short easy swim same day) + possibly a second quality session. Distribution: 80% easy, 20% hard. Only sustainable in windows — not as a default.

The choice between levels is a weekly decision, not a phase decision. An athlete might be Level 2 for six weeks, drop to Level 1 for a week when work explodes, go back to Level 2 for two weeks, then hit Level 3 for the three peak weeks of their Build block. That's normal. That's the system working as designed.

How a coach actually uses the levels

Sunday night (or Monday morning), the coach and athlete answer three questions: (1) How was last week's recovery — sleep, soreness, mood? (2) What does the calendar look like this week — work crunch, travel, family demands? (3) Where is the athlete in their block — base-building, threshold push, taper?

High stress + poor recovery → Level 1, accept it, no guilt. Normal stress + good recovery → Level 2, default. Low stress + great recovery + peak block window → Level 3. The level changes; the anchors do not.

The four anchors — what gets protected first

Every training week — regardless of level — has the same four anchor sessions. When life gets tight and the week shrinks from Level 3 down to Level 1, you don't randomly cut workouts. You cut filler and you keep the anchors. These four are how the athlete keeps moving forward even on the worst weeks.

1. Long ride
(usually Saturday)		 Aerobic engine, bike pacing, fueling rehearsal. The biggest single time investment of the week and the largest single fitness driver for long-course racing. Z1 (mostly) with Z2 touches in Build/Peak. Race-pace efforts in Peak. 2.5-3.5 h (half-iron focus) · 3.5-4.5 h (full-iron focus) · 1.5-2.5 h (other endurance)
2. Long run
(usually Sunday, ~24h after the long ride for the brick effect)		 Run durability, mental toughness, running on tired legs. Where races are won or lost. Z1 (mostly), Z2 in Build/Peak, race-pace touches in Peak. 75-90 min (half-iron) · 90-120 min (full-iron) · runners scale to event
3. Quality session
(usually Tuesday or Thursday)		 Block-specific stimulus. In Build, this is Z3 threshold work. In Peak, this is VO2 Max or race-pace. In Base, it may not exist yet. Z3 in Build · VO2 Max in Peak · skip in Base/Recovery 45-60 min total session (including warm-up / cool-down)
4. Easy frequency
(2-3 short sessions, anywhere they fit)		 Volume and recovery without fatigue cost. Most fitness is built here. Spread across the week. ZR-Z1 only. Conversational. If the athlete can't talk in full sentences, they're not in the right zone. 30-60 min each, 2-3× per week
When life gets crazy: cut everything, keep these four

A normal Level 2 week is the four anchors + filler. A Level 1 week is just the four anchors — possibly shortened. Never cut an anchor before cutting filler. An athlete who skips Tuesday's threshold session but does Saturday's long ride keeps moving forward. An athlete who does Tuesday's threshold and skips Saturday's long ride does not.

The session-prioritization decision tree

When a coach has to triage a broken week, the order of protection is fixed:

  1. Long ride goes first on the calendar. Lose this and you lose the largest single fitness stimulus of the week.
  2. Long run goes second — and ideally 24 hours after the long ride for the brick effect. Lose this and run durability lags.
  3. Quality session goes third. This is the block-specific stimulus. In a Recovery week, it disappears entirely — that's correct.
  4. Easy frequency goes fourth — but it's the first thing cut when life shrinks the week. Volume from easy sessions is replaceable; the anchor stimulus is not.

If the week's so tight that even one anchor has to go, the coach drops the quality session first, then halves the long run, then halves the long ride. The long ride is the last thing to fall.

Brick Training Workout type

A brick is a bike session immediately followed by a run, with no real recovery between them. The name comes from how the legs feel — bricks — for the first kilometre of the run. For triathletes, this is the single most important workout type that doesn't get its own block: it's how the body learns the race-day skill of running well on bike-fatigued legs.

Coaches working with runners and cyclists who aren't crossing modalities can mostly skip this section. Tri coaches and AG triathletes should treat it as required reading.

The physiology — what changes the moment you step off the bike

Understanding why bricks feel terrible the first time you do them makes the protocol obvious. Four things shift at once:

  1. Blood flow redistributes (horizontal → vertical). On the bike, you've been horizontal for hours with steady, supported blood flow. The instant you stand up, gravity pools blood in the legs and the heart has to fight to re-circulate. The first 30-60 seconds off the bike: dizziness, light-headedness, sometimes a brief HR spike. With practice, this clears in seconds instead of a minute.
  2. Muscle recruitment shifts (quads → glutes / hamstrings). Cycling is quad-dominant. Running is glute- and hamstring-dominant. When the athlete starts running, the glutes and hams are asked to fire hard while they're still cold and under-recruited, and the quads — fatigued — can't help. That mismatch is the "jelly legs" feeling.
  3. Neuromuscular lag. The nervous system takes time to switch firing patterns. The first 5-15 minutes of running off the bike, recruitment is sloppy. Cadence drops. Form falls apart. With practice, the lag shortens — at first 15 minutes, eventually 5 minutes, on race day sometimes 2 minutes.
  4. HR rises for the same metabolic cost. Running is more thermally and mechanically demanding than cycling at equivalent intensity. HR can jump 10-20 bpm in the first kilometre even though pace and RPE haven't changed. This is normal — and a perfect demonstration of why ECHO uses HR as a verifier, not a primary anchor.

The three stages of brick training

Bricks are progressed through three stages across a season. Doing race-simulation bricks in Base week 2 is how athletes get hurt; doing easy-easy bricks in race week is how athletes show up under-rehearsed. Match the stage to the block.

Stage When (block) Frequency Format What you're teaching
1. Adaptation Base 1 brick every 2-3 weeks 60-90 min Z1 bike → immediate 15 min Z1 run The transition itself. Body learns the blood-flow and recruitment shift in a low-stress setting. No intensity, no expectation.
2. Rehearsal Build 1 brick per week 60-90 min Z2 bike → immediate 20-30 min Z2 run Steady-state run off a controlled bike. Athlete proves they can hold race-adjacent pace on tired legs.
3. Race simulation Peak 1-2 per week, usually replacing a separate bike+run day Race-pace bike → immediate race-pace run. Durations approach race-distance fractions (e.g., 2h bike + 40min run for half-iron focus). Full execution under race conditions: pacing, fueling timing, transition logistics, the mental moment of starting the run. By the time race day comes, none of this is new.

The four brick rules — non-negotiable

  1. No walking. Get off the bike, change shoes (or don't), and start running within 30 seconds. Walking breaks the adaptation — the whole point is teaching the body to transition under fatigue. The only exception: if the athlete is truly broken (not "feels heavy" — broken), walk a bit and end the session early.
  2. First-kilometre discipline. The first 0.5-1.5 km will feel slow and heavy. Expect it. Do not speed up to "wake the legs up" — that's how the run blows up. Run easy until the legs come back (usually by km 1.5-2), then settle into the prescribed pace.
  3. Practice the transition exactly as race day. Same shoes, same towel layout, same nutrition. If the athlete races in cycling shoes off the bike, they practice in cycling shoes. If they swap to flats, they practice the swap. Race-day is not the day to rehearse logistics.
  4. Final bike fuel is taken 10-15 minutes before the dismount. Not 2 minutes before. Carbohydrate eaten right before transition doesn't reach the bloodstream until the athlete is already 5-10 minutes into the run, which is exactly when it's needed. Fuel taken at the wrong moment is fuel wasted, or worse — fuel that hits a vertical, jostling stomach and comes back up.
Bricks are fatiguing — replace, don't add

A brick is harder than a separate bike session plus a separate run session of equivalent volume. Do not add bricks on top of normal training. Replace the Tuesday hard bike + Thursday long run with a single Tuesday brick + a Thursday easy session. Coaches who layer bricks on top of full training plans produce broken athletes.

The Stress Budget & Plan A / Plan B When life happens

The single hardest lesson for a new coach: training stress is not the only stress your athlete is carrying. Work deadlines, kids' schedules, poor sleep, travel, emotional load — all of it draws from the same recovery budget that training draws from. A plan that ignores total load and only counts training hours is a plan that will break the athlete the first month it meets real life.

Total stress = training + work + family + sleep + emotional load

Every human has a finite recovery capacity per week. Call it 100 units, for simplicity. Training claims some. Work claims some. Family claims some. Sleep repays some (good sleep) or borrows some (poor sleep). Emotional load claims some. When the total exceeds capacity, adaptation stops and the athlete moves toward illness, injury, or burnout.

This is why the same 12-hour training week feels easy in March and crushing in May. The training didn't change. The rest of the budget did.

Week type Approximate load profile Coaching call
Calm week Work moderate, sleep solid (7-8 h), family stable, training 10-12 h. Total well under budget. Level 2 default. Consider Level 3 if this is a peak window of the Build/Peak block.
Loaded week Work spike (deadline, travel) or family demand (sick kid, partner traveling) or sleep dip (5-6 h, fragmented). One major non-training stressor. Drop to Level 2 if you were Level 3 — or Level 1 if you were Level 2. Cut filler, keep anchors.
Crisis week Multiple non-training stressors stack: work emergency + sick child + poor sleep + travel all in the same window. Level 1 only. No intensity, no goal workouts, no hero efforts. The job this week is to keep the athlete alive in the sport — not move them forward. Forward starts again next week.

The five stress types — and how each one degrades recovery differently

  • Work deadlines. Mental load activates cortisol, which interferes with sleep and recovery for days after the deadline ends. Even when the calendar clears, recovery lags by 1-2 weeks. Coach response: drop volume during the deadline; ramp slowly after.
  • Poor sleep. This is the highest-impact stressor most coaches under-weight. Sleep is when adaptation actually happens. Five-hour-night weeks aren't training weeks; they're cortisol weeks. Coach response: aggressive volume cut. If sleep doesn't recover, training has no destination.
  • Family disruption. Sick kids, aging parents, partner illness, family emergencies. Time is fragmented, sleep is broken, emotional load is high. Coach response: minimal training, no guilt, full presence with family. The fitness will be there in three weeks. The relationship damage from being absent during a crisis is not as easy to undo.
  • Travel. New time zone, hotel beds, disrupted meal timing, limited gear. Coach response: travel weeks become run-heavy weeks (running is always available). Skip the bike and pool if access is hard. Accept the dip.
  • Emotional / mental stress. Anxiety, grief, relationship strain, life uncertainty. Often the most invisible stressor — and the most destructive to recovery. Coach response: easy training only (Z1 movement can be therapeutic), no intensity. Suggest the athlete talk to someone if the load is sustained.

Plan A vs Plan B — write both in advance

The most useful planning move a coach makes is writing two versions of every week before the week starts. Plan A is the ideal version. Plan B is the survival version. The athlete doesn't choose between them on Monday — they choose throughout the week based on how recovery is actually going.

Plan A — calm life Plan B — busy life
Level Level 2 or Level 3 Level 1 or Level 2
Sessions All four anchors + filler + possibly a second quality session Four anchors only — possibly shortened (e.g., long ride from 4h to 2.5h)
Intensity Block-appropriate (Z3 work in Build, VO2 Max in Peak) Reduced or cut entirely; all Z1-Z2 in a real Plan B week
Training priority Priority #1-2 in the athlete's life this week Priority #3-4; work and family come first
Mental frame Build week — push the adaptation Maintenance week — keep the body moving and the habit alive

A common coaching mistake is treating Plan B as the failure version. It isn't. Plan B is the version that keeps the athlete in the sport through a crisis week. A coach who can move an athlete from Plan A to Plan B on a Wednesday — without drama, without guilt, without rebuilding the relationship from scratch — is doing the actual work of coaching adults with real lives. The athlete who survives 12 weeks on a mix of Plan A and Plan B weeks finishes the race. The athlete who tries to force Plan A through every week breaks in week 5.

The missed-session decision tree

Sessions get missed. That's not a problem — that's life. The problem is the catch-up reflex. Athletes who try to "make up" a missed week by doing a hero week the following week do not get fit faster. They get broken faster. The rule is the same at every duration: resume, don't make up.

Stability — the Hidden Driver of Durability Theory

Two athletes train the same total volume over 10 weeks — 100 hours each. One ends the period stronger, healthier, and ready for a big race. The other ends fatigued, plateaued, and probably nursing a niggle. The difference is almost never total volume. It's stability — how consistent the week-to-week training load actually is.

The stability index

Stability is the week-to-week variance in training load. A simple working definition for coaches:

Stability rule of thumb

Aim for under 10% week-to-week variance in training hours during a normal block. If this week is 10 hours, next week should land between 9 and 11 — not 5 and not 15. Recovery weeks are the planned exception (~50-65% of normal); those are scheduled, not random.

Why does this matter so much? Because the body adapts to consistent stimulus, not to the average of wildly different stimuli. An athlete who does 10h / 5h / 15h / 6h / 14h has the same 10-hour average as the athlete who does 10h / 10.5h / 9.5h / 11h / 10h — but the first athlete's body never gets a chance to actually adapt. It's constantly responding to a new shock or de-training back to baseline.

The four consistency patterns coaches see

Every age-group athlete falls into one of four patterns. Three of them are problems. One of them is the goal.

Pattern What it looks like Body response Outcome
The textbook plan
Rare		 Every week hits exactly to plan. Smooth linear progression. Zero life interruptions. Excellent adaptation — when it happens. Almost no one sustains this. Don't plan as if your athlete will. Plan for the messy version.
Life happens
Common — most age-groupers		 Solid weeks alternated with Plan B weeks. Forward overall trend with realistic ups and downs. Good adaptation, slower than textbook but sustained. Workable. This is what most successful athletes actually look like. The trajectory matters more than any single week.
Weekend warrior
Dangerous		 Almost nothing Monday-Thursday. Massive volume Friday-Sunday. Boom-and-bust spikes every week. Body never adapts — it's constantly recovering from spikes or de-training in the gaps. High injury risk. Burnout or injury before race day. Frequent. Almost every athlete who comes to a coach with chronic IT band, Achilles, or knee issues is running this pattern.
Steady
The goal		 Similar volume every week (under 10% variance). Same anchor sessions in the same windows. Filler flexes; structure doesn't. Adaptation compounds. Each week's gains build on the last. Recovery markers stay green. Injury risk low. The pattern of athletes who PR, who qualify, who stay healthy across multiple seasons.

How to build stability into a real schedule

  1. Design the weekly structure once. Lock the anchors into specific days and time windows: long ride Saturday 7-10 AM; long run Sunday 7-8:30 AM; quality session Tuesday 5:30 PM; easy frequency in whatever gaps exist. Then repeat that exact frame every week.
  2. Flex inside the frame, not around it. When life shrinks the week, Tuesday's quality session might shrink from 60 min to 40 min — but it still happens, in the same window, on the same day. The structure survives; the dose flexes.
  3. Use levels — not session-swapping — to absorb life stress. A coach who responds to a busy week by moving Saturday's long ride to Wednesday and Sunday's long run to Friday has destabilized the schedule. A coach who responds by keeping Saturday's long ride but cutting it from 3.5h to 2h has kept stability intact. Same anchor, smaller dose.
  4. Cap the upside in calm weeks. The temptation in a calm week after a hard one is to add an extra long session "since I have time." Resist. A 12h week followed by a 16h week followed by a 9h week is more destabilizing than three steady 12h weeks. Save the upside for the planned peak window.

Metrics Dashboard — TSS, CTL, ATL, TSB Data literacy

Most age-group athletes — and a lot of newer coaches — either ignore training-load metrics entirely or treat them as gospel. Neither extreme works. The four metrics below are tools for asking better questions, not verdicts on whether the athlete is doing well. Used honestly, they cut weeks of guessing out of the coaching process.

The four numbers and what each one is for

Metric What it measures Time window Typical range
TSS
Training Stress Score		 The load of a single workout. Combines duration and intensity into one number. Computed by the platform from power/pace/HR vs the athlete's threshold anchor. Per session 50 = easy 1h · 100 = hard 1h or moderate 2h · 200+ = very hard or very long
CTL
Chronic Training Load		 The 42-day rolling average of daily TSS. The "fitness bank." Moves slowly — it takes weeks of consistent work to build CTL, and weeks of detraining to lose it. ~6 weeks 40-100 for most endurance athletes. CTL ≈ 60 means the athlete averages ~60 TSS / day or ~420 TSS / week.
ATL
Acute Training Load		 The 7-day rolling average of daily TSS. The "fatigue debt." Moves fast — one hard week spikes it, one easy week brings it down. 1 week 30-100. Tracks current fatigue, not fitness.
TSB
Training Stress Balance		 CTL minus ATL. The "freshness" score. Positive = recovered relative to the chronic load; negative = currently fatigued. This is the actionable number. Live -50 to +50. Bands below.

The TSB bands — how to actually use the number

TSB range Athlete state Coach response
+10 or higher Very fresh. Possibly under-loaded. Race-ready territory in the final taper days. Good day for a hard session. If sustained for weeks without intent, the athlete is detraining — add load.
+5 to +10 Fresh, ready for hard work. Green light on Z3 / VO2 Max sessions. Race-pace work lands well here.
0 to +5 Neutral. Normal mid-week state during a Build block. Normal sessions as prescribed. No special action.
0 to -10 Slightly fatigued. Normal late in a hard week. Normal sessions still okay, but flag the trend. If TSB doesn't recover by the weekend, the week was too much.
-10 to -20 Fatigued. Body is in deficit. Drop a quality session this week. Replace it with easy. Watch recovery markers (sleep, mood, morning HR). Re-check TSB in 3-4 days.
Below -20 Very fatigued. Adaptation is no longer happening. Injury and illness risk both elevated. Take 3-7 days easy (Level 1, ZR-Z1 only). No intensity. Re-check TSB. If still under -20 after the easy period, there's a deeper issue — sleep, life stress, illness, or overtraining — investigate before resuming.

Reading the dashboard like a coach, not a robot

The metrics tell you what happened. They do not tell you why — and the why is where coaching lives. Three rules:

  1. Trend matters more than any single day. TSB at -15 after a hard Saturday is normal. TSB drifting from -5 to -10 to -15 over three weeks while volume hasn't changed is a problem. Look at the slope, not the point.
  2. The number doesn't tell you the cause. A negative TSB during a planned overreach week is exactly correct. A negative TSB during a planned recovery week is a warning sign — either the athlete didn't recover, or non-training stress is high, or the calibration is off (stale CP, stale CS, garbage TSS values). Always ask which one.
  3. Subjective markers outrank the dashboard when they disagree. If TSB says +12 but the athlete is sleeping poorly, dreading sessions, and waking up tired, the athlete is right and the dashboard is wrong. The dashboard is a model. The athlete is the system the model is trying to describe.
When TSS values lie

TSS is computed from the athlete's threshold anchors — CP for bike, CS for run / swim, LTHR for HR-only sessions. If those anchors are stale (last tested 12+ weeks ago, fitness has shifted), every TSS calculation since the last update is wrong, which means CTL and ATL are wrong, which means TSB is wrong. The first move whenever the metrics seem off-pattern is to ask when did we last update CP / CS / LTHR? Per ECHO, the retest cadence is every 6-8 weeks during active training.

CP : VO2 Ratio — Personalized Block Sequencing Advanced

The generic Base → Build → Peak → Taper sequence works for the average athlete. But coaches who only know the generic sequence will mis-program the athletes at either end of the physiological spectrum — and those athletes are the ones who came looking for a coach precisely because the generic plans haven't worked for them.

The diagnostic tool: the CP : VO2 Max ratio. It tells the coach which system to develop first, before sequencing the blocks the standard way.

ECHO compliance check

This section uses ECHO's block vocabulary (Base, Build, Peak, Taper, Recovery) only. It does not introduce new block types or reorder them in conflict with the ECHO Manual — it adjusts duration and emphasis within the standard sequence based on the athlete's physiological profile. ECHO retains full authority over block selection and progression rules.

What the ratio measures

CP : VO2 Max ratio = Critical Power divided by VO2 Max power.

  • CP is the highest sustainable power in a quasi-steady state — the threshold output. It is the bike anchor for all zone work.
  • VO2 Max power is the peak aerobic power output — what the athlete can produce for a few minutes at maximal aerobic effort.
  • The ratio between them tells the coach where the athlete's current weakness lives: is their threshold weak relative to their ceiling, or is their ceiling weak relative to their threshold?

Example: CP = 280 W, VO2 Max power = 350 W → ratio = 0.80. That's an anaerobic profile — high peak, lower sustainable. Different programming than an athlete at CP 270 W / VO2 Max 305 W (ratio 0.89, aerobic profile — sustainable ceiling, lower peak).

The three profiles and the programming response

Profile Ratio What it means How the standard block sequence adjusts
Anaerobic profile Below 0.83 High peak power, weak threshold. Often a former cyclist or runner with sprint or short-event history. Can produce a big watt for 3-5 min but can't sustain hard work for 30-60 min — exactly what long-course racing demands. Extend Build. Add 1-2 weeks of threshold-heavy Build before moving into Peak. Goal: raise CP first; let VO2 Max work in Peak land on a higher floor. Skipping this and going straight to Peak / VO2 Max work wastes the ceiling work — the threshold isn't strong enough to support it.
Balanced profile 0.83 to 0.87 Threshold and peak are proportional. Most well-rounded endurance athletes land here. Standard sequence. Base → Build → Peak → Taper as written. The textbook progression is right for this athlete.
Aerobic profile Above 0.87 Strong threshold relative to peak. Often a long-time endurance athlete with deep aerobic development but limited top-end. Can sustain steady work for hours but lacks the ceiling for late-race surges or short-course racing. Shift VO2 Max work earlier in the build year. Insert a focused VO2 Max emphasis inside Build (not waiting for Peak) to raise the ceiling, then transition into a threshold-maintenance Build, then standard Peak / Taper. Skipping ceiling work and only doing more threshold work makes the imbalance worse.

Why this matters for long-course coaching

Long-course racing is overwhelmingly a Base + threshold game. The peak-power ceiling matters less than for short-course racing because the athlete is never asked to produce maximal aerobic power for more than seconds at a time. But the ratio still matters — because where the athlete is currently weak determines what they need now, regardless of what the eventual race demands.

An anaerobic-profile half-iron athlete needs threshold work before they need long endurance work, because their current limiter is sustainable power, not peak power and not aerobic capacity. An aerobic-profile half-iron athlete might actually benefit from a short VO2 Max emphasis early in the year just to raise the ceiling enough that threshold work has somewhere to grow into.

Get the ratio honestly or don't use it

The ratio only works if both CP and VO2 Max power are measured cleanly. CP comes from the standard ECHO 3 / 6-or-8 / 12-min protocol. VO2 Max power is harder to measure honestly without lab testing — a field-test 5-min maximal effort is a reasonable proxy when the athlete is fresh, fueled, and motivated. If either number is sketchy (stale, fatigued, badly paced), the ratio is meaningless and the standard sequence is the safer default.

Time Design, the Family Talk & Collaborative Training The human side

Every coaching tool in this manual so far — zones, blocks, anchors, levels, the stress budget, the dashboard — fails if the athlete's life isn't structured to deliver the training. The most common reason age-groupers fall off training plans isn't lack of fitness, lack of motivation, or even injury. It's that they tried to find time for training in a life that didn't make room for it. The fix is structural, and it starts before the first workout.

Design the time — don't try to find it

The default human approach to fitting in training: wake up, look at the day, hope a window opens up. That approach loses to every other priority in the athlete's life — meetings, kids, errands, decision fatigue, the couch — every single day. The structural alternative:

  1. Lock the anchors first, on the calendar, before anything else. Saturday 7-10 AM = long ride. Sunday 7-8:30 AM = long run. Tuesday 5:30-6:30 PM = quality session. Thursday 5:30-6:15 PM = quality run or swim. These are calendar events, not intentions. They get the same treatment as a doctor's appointment.
  2. Fill the gaps with easy frequency. 30-minute easy run before work. Lunch-break swim. Easy spin after dinner. Easy sessions don't need protected windows; they fit into the cracks that already exist in the calendar.
  3. Make the structure repetitive across weeks. Same anchor sessions, same days, same windows, week after week. Predictability removes decision fatigue. By week 4, the athlete isn't choosing whether to train at 5:30 AM on Tuesday — they're just doing what Tuesday is. That's the goal.

Constraints clarify priorities

Coaches who've worked with both unlimited-time athletes and severely time-constrained athletes know a counterintuitive truth: the constrained athletes often train better. Eight hours of forced focus beats fifteen hours of unfocused fitting-it-in. Limits force the athlete to:

  • Cut filler. Every session has to earn its slot.
  • Simplify logistics. Same gear, same fuel, same route. No decision tax on workout day.
  • Travel-proof the week. When the bike or pool isn't available, the runnable session always is. Build a fallback by default.
  • Sync with family rhythms. The training windows are the ones the family already accepts; conflict drops.

A useful coaching reframe when an athlete complains they don't have enough time: "You're probably right. Let's design eight focused hours instead of trying to find twelve." The athlete who trains eight purposeful hours per week for twelve consecutive weeks finishes ahead of the athlete who tries to train fifteen and disappears in week six.

The Family Talk — the conversation that has to happen before week one

Resentment is the single biggest reason age-group athletes quit training mid-build. Not injury. Not fitness plateau. Resentment — from the partner who didn't sign up for Saturday-morning solo parenting, from the kids who keep getting told "after my run," from the athlete themselves who feels guilty every Sunday afternoon. The conversation that prevents resentment is short, deliberate, and happens before any training plan begins.

A working script — the opener

Sit down with the partner / family at a calm moment (not while cooking, not while watching the kids, not at the end of a stressful day). Open with:

"I want to train for [race / event]. It matters to me. But not at the cost of us. I want us to figure this out together. What are your questions? What concerns you?"

Then listen. Don't defend. The real concerns will surface: "Will you be present when you're home?" "Are we losing our weekends?" "Why does this matter to you?" "Is this safe?" Each one gets an honest answer, not a deflection.

The conversation has three jobs: (1) make the time commitment visible and bounded (8-12 weeks, ~10 hours per week, mostly early morning); (2) set explicit untouchable family time zones (e.g., Friday night, Saturday afternoon, Sunday dinner — no training, no exceptions); (3) ask the partner directly when they can support a hard week and when they can't, so the level decisions later have buy-in.

Untouchable family windows — written down, honored without exception

The most powerful move from the Family Talk: explicit, named, honored family windows where training never happens. Examples coaches see work:

  • Weekday dinners. Home by 6:30 PM, no training after, every weekday. Partner and kids see the athlete at the dinner table every night — that single fact dissolves most weekday resentment.
  • Friday night = date night. No training, no early bedtime for tomorrow's long ride. The partner gets one inviolable couple night every week.
  • Saturday afternoon = family time. Long ride ends by 10 or 11 AM. The rest of Saturday is family. Long-ride days are also family days.
  • Sunday evenings. No training after the long run. Sunday from noon onward belongs to the family.

The windows aren't suggestions. They're structural. The athlete doesn't decide each week whether to honor them — they're already in the calendar, in red, with the same protection level as the anchor workouts. Both blocks of time get treated as non-negotiable.

Collaborative training — integrate, don't isolate

The hidden risk of an early-morning + weekend-anchor structure: the athlete trains entirely alone, the family sees fitness as the thing that takes the athlete away from them. The fix is collaborative training — sessions designed to include family or friends, scheduled deliberately:

  • Easy runs with kids on bikes / scooters. 30-45 min on safe routes. Counts as Z1 volume; reads as family time.
  • Social rides with coffee stops. Easy bike with a friend or partner, planned café stop halfway. Z1 the whole way. Athlete gets the volume; partner gets the connection.
  • Family walks for active recovery. 30-45 min after dinner, conversational pace. Counts as ZR-Z1 movement; reads as evening family time.

One or two collaborative sessions per week — particularly during easy weeks or recovery weeks — moves the family's experience of training from "the thing that takes them away" to "the thing they're part of." Partners of athletes who get this right are usually the ones at the finish line.

Coaching note — the integration question

A useful question to ask new athletes in the first or second session: "What's one easy session this week we could design so your partner / kid is part of it?" The answer tells the coach a lot about whether the athlete sees training as something they're doing with their life or against it. Athletes who can't think of one are usually the ones who quit in week five.

ECHO doesn't directly cover the human-side logistics of family integration — that's traditionally treated as outside the coaching scope. Foundations explicitly includes it because, in practice, no zone system or block sequence survives a training relationship the family resents. The four-anchor / level-based system in this manual works because it makes room for the conversation; if the conversation never happens, the system breaks for human reasons before it has a chance to break for physiological ones.

Fueling & Hydration

Fueling is not separate from training — it is training. Every session is the work plus the fueling that supported it. A friend's gel strategy. Ten conflicting articles. Mile 70 hits and a marathon turns into a walk. Months of training, undone in an afternoon.

The fix isn't a better guess. It's math against your body — your sweat rate, your sodium loss, your race duration, your gut. This section gives you the foundations you need so the numbers later make sense: the Power of 3 framework, your sweat test, your sodium target, and your carbohydrate window. Stop guessing. Get dialed.

The ranges below are the published, defensible starting points for any athlete. Your numbers — the per-hour grams, the per-leg sodium, the bottle-by-bottle plan — come from the Race Day Fuel Planner, which builds the math against your body, your gut, your race. This section teaches you the why so you can own the how.

The Power of 3

Three things, in this order — every session, every race:

  1. Fluid — the water you replace as you sweat.
  2. Sodium — the primary electrolyte you lose in sweat. Sodium keeps the fluid usable.
  3. Carbohydrate — the fuel your muscles and your brain run on.

Order matters. Fluid is first because nothing else works without it — sodium can't move and carbs can't absorb if you're behind on water. Sodium is second because drinking a lot of plain water without sodium dilutes your blood sodium and actually makes you feel worse (hyponatremia at the extreme). Carbohydrate is third — it does the heaviest metabolic work, but only with the fluid and sodium underneath it in place.

This is also the order you fix problems in. If a session goes sideways, audit fluid first, sodium second, carbs third. Most fueling problems are fluid problems wearing a different costume.

Hydration — find YOUR sweat rate

Generic hydration guidance is wrong for almost everyone. Sweat rate varies enormously between athletes, between hot and cool days, between hard and easy sessions, between cycling and running. The only honest answer is the one you measure.

The sweat test — how to do it

  1. Empty your bladder.
  2. Weigh yourself naked on a digital scale. Record it.
  3. Train for exactly one hour at a typical intensity. Track every ounce of fluid you drink.
  4. Towel off all sweat.
  5. Weigh yourself naked again. Record it.

The math

Sweat rate per hour = (pre-weight − post-weight) + fluid consumed

1 kg of body-weight loss ≈ 1 L of sweat. 1 lb ≈ 16 fl oz. Add the fluid you drank.

Worked example. You start at 70.0 kg. You ride one hour, drink 600 ml. You finish at 69.2 kg. Weight lost = 0.8 kg = 800 ml of sweat. Plus 600 ml consumed = 1,400 ml/hr sweat rate for that intensity, that day.

Replacement target

You don't replace 100% of what you lose — the gut can't absorb that fast and chasing it causes sloshing, bloating, and worse. Aim to finish a normal training session no more than 2% lighter than you started. Same for race day. Typically that means replacing roughly 60–80% of your sweat losses while you work.

Retest seasonally. Heat acclimation, fitness, and the season itself all shift sweat rate. Test in early summer, again mid-season, again when the cold settles in. A once-a-year number is rarely the right one for today.

Sodium

Sodium is what makes fluid usable. It pulls water into your bloodstream and out of your gut, holds blood volume, and keeps muscles and nerves firing. Sweat carries roughly 400 mg to 1,500+ mg of sodium per litre depending on the athlete. Heavy sweaters and salty sweaters (white crust on dark kit) sit at the high end.

The formula behind your sodium target

The Race Day Fuel Planner uses this exact formula to set your per-hour sodium:

mg/hr = sweat rate (L/hr) × sodium concentration (mg/L) × replacement factor

  • Replacement factor 0.75 — default. Protective; you don't need to chase 1:1.
  • 0.85 — for athletes with occasional cramping history.
  • 1.0 — reserved for gut-trained, high-saltiness athletes racing in heat. Not the default.

Hard floor: 300 mg/hr (anyone sweating needs some). Hard ceiling: 1,500 mg/hr regardless of what the formula says.

The honest starting range for endurance work is 500–1,000 mg/hr, climbing for heat, heavy/salty sweaters, and long course. Most commercial sports drinks sit at the low end (200–400 mg/L of mix) — long-course athletes typically supplement with electrolyte tablets, salt capsules, or higher-concentration mixes to get there.

Failure modes. Too little sodium: heavy late-session fatigue, cramping, nausea, brain fog, swollen-feeling hands or face. Hyponatremia at the extreme. Too much sodium without fluid: stomach upset and nausea — rarely the actual issue unless you're dosing salt without drinking.

Carbohydrate — the engine fuel

Carbohydrates are the body's preferred energy source during endurance work. Stored as glycogen in muscle and liver — a finite reserve, roughly 90–120 minutes at moderate intensity before it runs low. Fat covers the rest at easy intensities; above threshold, you're running almost entirely on carbohydrate. Long-course racing lives or dies on the carbohydrate strategy. When glycogen runs out — "bonking," "hitting the wall" — sustainable pace drops dramatically, the brain gets foggy, and the day shifts to damage control.

The trained gut

Carbohydrate absorption is a trainable system. Athletes who routinely take 70–90 g/hr in long workouts genuinely tolerate higher race-day loads than athletes who only "eat" during competition. If you debut a high-carb strategy on race day, your gut isn't going to thank you. Build intake in training the same way you build fitness — progressively.

Multiple Transportable Carbohydrates (MTC)

At intakes above ~60 g/hr, the gut starts to hit the absorption ceiling for glucose alone. Mixing glucose with fructose — typically a 2:1 ratio — uses a second intestinal transporter and unlocks higher absorption rates. Most modern sports nutrition products are built on this principle. If you're targeting 80+ g/hr, you need MTC products. Body size is not the limiting factor; the absorption rate through the gut is, and that's similar across body sizes.

Carb sources — what counts as a gram

Read labels, not branding. A gram of carb is a gram of carb whether it's a gel, a drink mix, a chew, a banana, a rice cake, or a sports bar. What matters:

  • Total grams per hour — measured, not guessed.
  • Carbohydrate type — MTC (glucose + fructose) once you go above ~60 g/hr.
  • Form variety — a mix of liquid, gel/chew, and occasional real food sits better than four hours of one format.

The two non-negotiables

  1. Rehearse in training. Race day is not the day to try a new product, a new flavor, or a new intake rate. Long sessions are the rehearsal — the gut, the timing, the flavor fatigue, the bottle layout, all of it.
  2. Plan grams first, products second. Decide the math. Then assemble the products that hit the math. Most athletes who fade late didn't have a math problem — they had a plan problem.

Heat — when sodium and fluid both move

Heat changes everything. Sweat rate climbs (use the warm/hot columns above). Sodium target climbs with it. Add roughly +100 mg/hr sodium above the table values in warm and hot conditions. Cool the skin, not the gut — ice in your hat, on your neck, in your kit. Sipping a slushy or chewing ice chips is approved cooling; chugging ice-cold fluid is not (slows gastric emptying, risks GI distress). And: pre-cool when you can. The minutes before the gun matter.

Carb-loading — race week

For any race of marathon distance or longer, the published protocol is sustained T-2 = T-1 loading over 36–48 hours. Both days deliver the same carbohydrate target — no ramp-down on T-2. The mechanism is glycogen supercompensation: muscle and liver glycogen stores roughly double when high-carb intake is sustained for 36–48 hours before competition. A T-2 reduction blunts the supercompensation curve and leaves you starting the race under-loaded.

Tier targets (g of carbohydrate per kg of body weight per day):

  • Conservative — 8 g/kg/day (band 7–9). Opt-in: first-timer, GI-sensitive, or "I can't eat that much."
  • Standard (default) — 9 g/kg/day for marathons, 10 g/kg/day for 70.3 / Full Iron.
  • Aggressive — 11 g/kg/day. Experienced athletes targeting upper-end glycogen.
  • Advanced + Aggressive — 12 g/kg/day. Elite / sub-elite with proven gut tolerance.

Expect 1–3 lb of water-weight gain — every gram of glycogen holds 3–4 grams of water. That weight is fuel.

Spread carb intake across snacks throughout the day rather than cramming it into a few large meals — pretzels, granola bars, fruit, energy chews, sports drink, white rice, bagels, plain toast with honey or jam. The full race-morning food rules and the per-meal carb distribution (30/35/35 for T-2) are in the Race Day Fuel Planner.

The athlete-facing summary

  • Power of 3: fluid → sodium → carbohydrate. In that order. Always.
  • Know your sweat rate. Sweat test seasonally. Replace 60–80%. Finish no more than 2% lighter.
  • Sodium 500–1,000 mg/hr as the starting band — climbs with heat, heavy/salty sweat, and long course. Hard ceiling 1,500 mg/hr; hard floor 300 mg/hr.
  • Carb scales with race type — 40–60 g/hr Sprint, 60–90 Olympic, 80–100 Half Iron, 90–120 Full Iron (up to 140 g/hr advanced + aggressive). Marathon 60–90 g/hr.
  • MTC above ~60 g/hr. Glucose + fructose. Train the gut progressively.
  • Plan grams, not products. Math first. Brands second.
  • Rehearse everything in training. Race day is execution, not experimentation.

Mental Foundations

Training load, fueling, and recovery are the physical pillars. Mental skill is what determines whether all of that preparation actually shows up — in the long session, on the bad day, in the race that isn't going to plan, in the comeback after injury, in the third year of training when novelty stops doing the work for you.

Mental skill is a skill. You don't get a stronger aerobic engine by hoping for one, and you don't get a tougher mind by hoping for one either. This section covers the foundations every EchoDevo athlete should be working from. The deeper work — mindset archetypes, scenario-by-scenario coping responses, the 38-scenario coping matrix — lives in the full ECHO Coaching Manual and in the standalone Mental Mindset Course. Foundations first.

What we believe has power

What we believe shapes what we experience. Beliefs about ourselves, our abilities, the people around us — they have a profound effect on outcomes. This is one of the most important lessons of an athletic career: we have a choice in what we believe. We can entertain limiting, negative thoughts that hold us back, or we can consciously shift to more empowering beliefs.

The frame I keep coming back to: Happiness = Events − Expectations. When experience meets or exceeds expectation, we feel content. When it falls short, we feel disappointment. You can't always control the events. You can absolutely shape the expectations.

Going into a big race, the old default is to set expectations sky-high — "I have to win or I'm a failure." A better approach is balanced: you still have the big dreams, but you temper them with realism. You focus on the process, on doing your absolute best, on learning and growing — not just on the outcome. Even if you don't win, you can still feel proud of the effort. It's a step in the right direction.

Same with a bad training session. The old reflex is to beat yourself up and feel like a fraud. The shift is to acknowledge the frustration, then look for the lesson. What can I learn here? How can I use it to get better? What positive is hidden in the struggle? Often the answer is patience, problem-solving in the moment, or a small technical tell you'd otherwise have missed.

Maintaining an empowered mindset isn't easy and it isn't constant. It takes vigilance and practice. But when you harness it, there's no real limit to what you can achieve. Your beliefs don't just shape your athletic performance — they shape your whole life.

Embrace the unknown

No matter how much success you've had, the voice of doubt still asks: what if things go wrong? The freedom is in embracing the unknown rather than fighting it.

Many of us believe we have to keep being who we've always been. "I've always been shy, so I can't speak up." "I've never been good at swimming, so I never will be." Those self-imposed limits are usually rooted in past hurts and failures — memories that fuel the fear of the future. But here's the liberating truth: we are not bound by our past selves. Just because we've acted or felt a certain way before doesn't mean we're destined to keep doing it. Every day, every moment offers the choice of who to be.

The reality is none of us can truly predict what's coming. The future is unknown. Carrying around emotional baggage from the past becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy of negativity — we end up trying to survive instead of thrive. The shift is letting go of that frame and opening up to a more positive, adventurous outlook. Let's not trust the future based on what we know of the past.

Life is constant change. Seen through the right lens, that change is invigorating rather than intimidating. We can learn to trust the process of change and write our own story moving forward. Each day is a new opportunity to redefine ourselves, to challenge our preconceptions about what we're capable of. Today might be the day you sign up for the race you've been avoiding. Choose who you are every day. Your past does not dictate your future.

The "View Askew" — perspective in racing

Athletes get trapped in a View Askew: tunnel vision on the short term — the next race, the next workout, the next make-or-break moment. The intensity is valuable. The blinders are not.

Many of us operate as if progress is a linear, predictable path — stick to the plan, avoid disruption, keep improving. The reality is much messier. Chaos theory and the butterfly effect both make the same point: small actions compound. Every workout, every race, every choice creates a new version of you that keeps evolving. The setbacks and challenges aren't just obstacles — they're integral to the process. They're shaping you into the competitor you're meant to become.

The "Blank Mind" approach to racing

The most powerful way to transcend the View Askew is racing with a blank mind. Let go of preconceptions and expectations. Be present. Respond to what unfolds.

Tune out the inner critic. Tune out the external noise. Tap into intuition and adaptability. When you race with a blank mind you become more attuned to the shifts and dynamics unfolding around you — able to anticipate changes, seize opportunities, make split-second decisions that give you an edge. The opposite — the athlete fixated on a specific time or pace and unable to adapt to the chaos of competition — is the athlete who ends up sabotaging their own potential.

It's not a soft skill. It requires letting go of control and detachment from results — which is hard. But when an athlete reaches open, receptive focus, that's when the magic happens. Care deeply while still letting go. Fiercely compete while staying present. Embrace the striving, not just the arriving.

Don't lose yourself — competitive drive that serves you

The best athletes have an intense inner competitiveness — a fire that sometimes looks like obsession. That same powerful force that propels you forward can also start to work against you. It can shift from focused motivation into unhealthy obsession.

Sport psychology distinguishes two sources of competitive drive:

  • Hyper-competitiveness — chasing the constant validation of self-worth through winning and accolades. Fulfillment comes from outside. It's never enough; trophies don't actually satisfy. In the extreme it pushes people to cut corners, burn out, or break down.
  • True competitive motivation — driven from within. Growth. Discovery. The work itself. "Addicted to the process," not to the medal.

The greatest athletes don't sit purely on one side. They harness fiery external drive while ultimately competing for self-actualizing reasons. Even Jordan and Kobe — Jordan using perceived slights as fuel, Kobe stating he played "to learn." Mastery alongside fire. The transcendent ones grasp this: you have to care deeply while still letting go.

The practical question to come back to: Where is my competitiveness coming from today? External validation or internal mastery? The internal version is what lasts.

Goals, targets, and outcomes

Three things athletes routinely confuse. Confusing them is one of the most common reasons athletes lose motivation, peak too early, or crash after a race.

  • Outcomes are results you don't fully control. Win my age group. Qualify for Worlds. Sub-3 marathon. Outcomes depend on weather, competition, race-day execution, and luck. They're motivating to point at — but they're a bad place to put your daily attention.
  • Targets are performance benchmarks you mostly control. Run 1:25 for the half. Hold 250 W on the bike split. Swim 1:35/100 m. Concrete, measurable, achievable on a good day.
  • Goals — process goals — are the daily and weekly behaviors that make targets and outcomes possible. Five swims a week for six weeks. Don't miss the long ride. Eat the prescribed grams every Saturday. Sleep 8 hours on weeknights. Entirely within your control.

Outcomes ride on Targets. Targets ride on Goals. Goals ride on daily behavior. When athletes get stuck or burned out, it's almost always because they've inverted the pyramid — staring at outcomes every day instead of executing goals every day. Coach yourself the right way up.

Self-efficacy — the single most important mental variable

Self-efficacy is the working-memory-level conviction that the task in front of you is doable, and that you are the person who can do it. Not talent. Not deserving. Not motivation (which comes and goes). Once basic fitness is in place, self-efficacy is the most reliable single predictor of how an athlete will perform.

Built deliberately, from four sources:

  • Mastery experiences — actually doing the hard thing and surviving it. Every long session you finish, every interval set you complete, every race you execute is a deposit. There is no substitute for this.
  • Vicarious experiences — watching athletes you identify with succeed under similar conditions. Training partners. Your coach's other athletes. "If they can do it, I can do it."
  • Verbal persuasion — a coach or trusted partner telling you, clearly, that you're capable. Less powerful than the first two — but real.
  • Physiological state — interpreting your body's signals (nerves, elevated HR, butterflies) as readiness rather than as threat. Same physiology, different story.

The practical move: collect mastery. Keep a record of the hard sessions you executed cleanly. When confidence wobbles, read the record. It's much harder to talk yourself out of a thing you have already done.

Growth mindset — setbacks are information, not verdicts

A fixed mindset believes ability is mostly innate — you have it or you don't. A growth mindset believes ability is developed through effort, strategy, and time. Both voices live in every athlete. The question is which one is louder when the work gets hard.

Growth mindset under stress treats setbacks as information, not as verdicts. Asks "what did this teach me?" before "what does this mean about me?" Sees the harder version of the workout as the more interesting one. Compares today's effort to what's possible today, not to a peak day. Talks about progress as process, not as inherent talent.

Fixed-mindset signals: "I'm just not a fast runner." "I've never been good at swimming." "I don't have the genetics for this." Avoiding sessions where you might look bad. Treating one bad workout as evidence about identity rather than data about a day. Comparing yourself to others as a verdict on worth.

You will catch yourself in fixed-mindset thinking. Everyone does. The skill is noticing it, naming it, and reframing the same situation in growth-mindset terms. With practice, the growth frame becomes the default — but only with practice.

Intrinsic vs. extrinsic motivation

Intrinsic motivation — doing the work because the work itself matters. You like training. You like the long ride. You like getting better. The activity is its own reward.

Extrinsic motivation — doing the work for the external payoff. The medal. The podium. The Kona slot. The recognition. Real and sometimes powerful — but fragile. It evaporates the day the reward stops feeling worth the cost.

The athletes who last for years are the ones whose core is intrinsic, with extrinsic motivation layered on top as accelerant rather than as fuel. If the only reason you train is the medal, the season you don't get one becomes the season you quit. Every few months, ask yourself why. If the only honest answer is the result, fold a piece of process back in. Find something about the training itself that you love.

Daily reflection — three questions, two minutes

  1. What went well today? (Training, fueling, sleep, mood — anything.)
  2. What got in the way? (Sessions skipped, fueling missed, stress bleeding into the day — anything.)
  3. What is the one thing I'll do differently tomorrow? (Concrete. Singular. Doable.)

Don't write a journal. Write three answers. The reason it works isn't catharsis — it's pattern detection. After two weeks you start seeing what correlates with good sessions and bad ones. After two months you stop being surprised by your own performance because you can see the inputs.

Bring reflection notes to coach check-ins. They give your coach signal no power file can provide.

Gratitude — naming what's good while the hard things are also true

Endurance sport is voluntary, and it's hard. Every athlete eventually hits a stretch where the volume feels like a burden, the alarm clock like an enemy, and the long session ahead like a punishment. That's the moment gratitude becomes a performance tool, not a self-help cliché.

Gratitude is the deliberate practice of noticing what's working — not pretending hard things aren't hard. Naming what's still good while the hard things are also true. "This is a hard session AND I'm lucky to have a body that can do it." Both can be true. Holding both makes the session executable.

Simple practice: at the start of every long session, name one thing — out loud or in your head — you're grateful for. The body. The weather. A training partner. The sport itself. Ten seconds. It changes the next four hours.

Comparison — only against your own past self

Comparison to other athletes tells you almost nothing useful. It tells you nothing about your physiology, your training history, your life context, your response to load. It only says: someone else, with completely different inputs, produced a different output.

Social media has made this 10× worse. Every athlete now has an audience they didn't choose, watching highlights of strangers they don't know. We're not actually worried about what other people think — we're worried about what we think others think. That's self-doubt wearing a costume.

The only comparison that produces signal is comparison to your own past self. Stronger than six months ago? Fueling better than a year ago? More durable than last season? Those are answerable, informative, and within your control. When you catch yourself comparing to another athlete, ask one question: Does this person's training history, life, and physiology match mine? The honest answer is always no. Then put your attention back on your own data and your own next session.

Race-day mental skills — when it counts

Open-water anxiety and the "lump in the throat"

That panic-attack feeling 200 yards into a swim — heart racing, can't breathe, like there's something lodged in your throat — is real and it's common. The medical name is laryngospasm: an involuntary, reflexive tightening of the vocal cords triggered by cold water, swallowing water, or anxiety. Combined with race-day adrenaline, it can feel like a nightmare.

Three breathing techniques that work — practice them on dry land first:

  • Diaphragmatic breathing — hand on chest, hand on belly. Inhale through the nose; the belly should rise, the chest stay still. Exhale slowly through the mouth. This keeps the throat relaxed and reduces the chance of a spasm.
  • Straw breathing — picture breathing out through a narrow straw. Inhale through the nose, then exhale slowly through pursed lips as if through the straw. The long, slow exhale calms the nervous system.
  • Humming breaths — inhale through the nose. As you exhale, hum softly ("mmm"). The vibration directly relaxes the vocal cords. Can be done between strokes if you feel the tightness coming on.

Also: acclimate to cold water gradually. Don't dive into freezing water cold. Take breaks if the tightness shows up mid-race — roll to your back, breaststroke, regroup. Switching strokes is not a DNF.

The "open mindset" — Holly's story

One of my athletes had her first-ever DNF at a 12-hour trail race — got sick 20 miles in, fell multiple times. She came back the next year having changed her nutrition and her headlamp, but admitted she still wasn't entering the race with the open mindset she likes to have. "This race was more about redemption for last year." She set the course record anyway.

The lesson isn't that fear doesn't matter. The lesson is that execution beats narrative. Goal: 75 g/hr carbs, 750 ml/hr fluids, 1000 mg/hr sodium. She fell short of the targets as the race went on — but she didn't have a major energy dip. Plan in place, math against the body, executed honestly, even on a redemption story. That's the pattern.

Recovery is mental too

Every athlete eventually faces the elephants in the room of an offseason: I'll lose fitness. I'll gain weight. I'll get weaker. I can't take time off. I feel fine, I don't need it.

If this is you, let me be blunt: not taking a proper break is just plain dumb. Ignoring rest isn't just a bad idea — it's sabotaging your own potential. The science:

  • VO₂ max declines 4–6% after 10–14 days of inactivity (reduced plasma volume + slight cardiac output drop) — and rebounds quickly once training resumes.
  • Tissue repair takes 10–14 days. Tendons, ligaments, and bones repair micro-damage on a slower timeline than muscle.
  • Hormonal balance takes 10–14 days. Cortisol normalizes; anabolic hormones (testosterone, growth hormone) rise back to baseline.
  • Weight gain of 1–3 lb is fuel, not failure — glycogen replenishment holds 3–4 g of water per gram, and a small buffer protects against RED-S.
  • Mental detachment requires real distance — two weeks, not three days.

One week off isn't enough. Two weeks off is the most important training you'll do all year. If you only took one week, or skipped the offseason, run the 10-day reset: 7 days of full rest, then 3 days of low-intensity walking, hiking, or easy jogging. No structure. No pressure.
