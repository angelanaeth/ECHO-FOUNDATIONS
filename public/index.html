Foundations Manual

The intro-level coaching playbook for new EchoDevo coaches — before you graduate to the full ECHO Manual.

Welcome

Welcome to the EchoDevo Foundations Manual.

This manual is the entry point into the EchoDevo coaching system. It exists to bring new coaches up to speed on the language, the zones, the testing protocols, the block types, and the coaching philosophy that the full ECHO Coaching Manual is built on.

Foundations is intentionally simpler than ECHO. ECHO is a complete decision-making framework — it teaches coaches how to diagnose athletes, select training blocks, dose stress, and translate decisions into clear athlete communication. Foundations teaches the vocabulary and the foundational physiology that has to be in place before any of that decision-making makes sense.

Read this manual first. Then graduate to ECHO when you're ready.

How to Use This Manual

Foundations is organized in the order a new coach should learn the system:

  • Zones — the language of intensity. HR, power, pace, RPE, and how they connect.
  • Testing — CP and CS — how we establish an athlete's actual sustainable threshold instead of guessing.
  • Block Types — the building blocks of any training plan.
  • Fueling & Hydration — the Power of 3, the sweat test, and the basics of carbohydrate.
  • Mental Foundations — the mindset skills that determine whether physical fitness translates into performance.

Each section can be read on its own, but the manual is designed to be read in order. The zones don't make sense without testing, the blocks don't make sense without zones, and the fueling and mindset sections amplify everything else.

The Two Foundational Rules

Before any of the technical content matters, two principles sit underneath all of it.

1. Personalization — Train YOUR body, not a generic plan.
Every athlete has unique physiology, life context, training history, and response to load. A workout that builds one athlete will break another. The numbers we set — heart rate zones, power zones, paces, fueling targets — are derived from that specific athlete's testing and history, not from a chart in a magazine.

2. Use data and principles to make decisions.
Coaching is not guessing. We anchor every training and pacing decision in objective signals: Critical Power, Critical Speed, heart rate response, perceived exertion, and how the athlete is actually adapting week to week. Vibes are not a plan.

These two rules pull in opposite directions on purpose. Personalization keeps us from rigid template-coaching. Data keeps us from drifting into "whatever feels right." Holding both at the same time is what separates coaching from cheerleading.

Who Foundations Is For

Zones

Zones are the language of training. They turn a vague instruction like "go easy" or "push it" into something specific, measurable, and repeatable. Every workout in your plan will be prescribed in zones — so the goal of this section is simple: by the end of it, you should know what each zone means, how to find it, and how to know you're in it.

ECHO uses one shared 5-band system across every modality: ZR · Z1 · Z2 · Z3 · VO2 Max. The bands stay the same whether you're talking about heart rate, bike power, run pace, or swim pace — what changes is the anchor each modality is built from. This shared vocabulary is intentional: when your coach says "Z2 ride" or "Z3 run repeats," you should be able to hold those two ideas in the same shape in your head.

The four ways to measure effort

  • Heart rate (HR) — internal cost. Universal. Slightly delayed and influenced by heat, sleep, hydration, and stress.
  • Power (bike) — external output, instantaneous, no lag. The cleanest bike anchor when available.
  • Pace (run, swim) — external output expressed as time-per-distance. The cleanest run and swim anchor.
  • RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion) — subjective effort. Not a primary zone system in ECHO — it's the verification and safety-valve. If the output target says 250W but RPE says 10/10 on a Z2 day, the day is wrong, not the math.

You don't need all four to train well. The rule we work from is Anchor → Range → Verify: test the output anchor (CP for bike, CS for run and swim), build the zone ranges from that anchor, and verify internal cost with HR, breathing, and RPE. Then apply context — heat, terrain, fatigue, fueling — on top.

The bands (what they're for)

  • ZR — Recovery. Active regeneration, reset work, low-cost movement. Unlimited duration. If you finish a ZR session feeling worked, it wasn't ZR.
  • Z1 — Durability Base. Primary low-cost volume; chassis-building. Easy steady efforts in the 2-6+ hour range on the bike. This is where the aerobic engine and the durability foundation are built. The majority of your training year lives in ZR-Z1.
  • Z2 — Steady / Durable. Race-pace feel for long-course racing. Sustained aerobic work, race-adjacent control, 1-3 hours on the bike. Sub-threshold but real work — talking shrinks to short phrases.
  • Z3 — Threshold / Critical. Hard sustained efforts at or near threshold. 20-90 minutes total of work, usually delivered as intervals. This is where you train sustainable expression directly — the ceiling of "sustainable hard."
  • VO2 Max — Peak / Ceiling. Short maximal-aerobic intervals, typically 3-8 minutes. Builds the ceiling, not the floor. High cost, low repeatability, and only useful when the structure underneath it exists.

HR Zones — % of LTHR

LTHR is your Lactate Threshold Heart Rate: the heart rate you can hold for roughly one hour of all-out effort. It's not your max HR. Once we know your LTHR, every HR zone is a percentage of that number. (How we test for LTHR lives in Section 3.)

Canonical reference (live from ECHO): these values are auto-synced. Do not edit by hand.
  • ZR — Recovery — ≤ 76% of LTHR
  • Z1 — Durability Base — 76-86% of LTHR
  • Z2 — Steady / Durable — 86-93% of LTHR
  • Z3 — Threshold / Critical — 93-100% of LTHR
  • VO2 Max — HR is a lagging confirmation only. HR cannot reliably govern VO2 work because it ramps too slowly to catch short intervals. Use power or pace, with RPE as the cross-check.

Bike Power Zones — % of CP

CP (Critical Power) is the highest power you can sustain in a quasi-steady state — the horizontal asymptote of your power-duration curve. CP is the bike anchor for every zone, every interval target, and every pacing decision. Section 3 covers the 3 / 6 / 12-minute test we use to find it.

Canonical reference (live from ECHO):
  • ZR — Recovery — < 65% of CP · unlimited duration
  • Z1 — Durability Base — 65-79% of CP · 2-6+ hour efforts
  • Z2 — Steady / Durable — 79-89% of CP · 1-3 hours
  • Z3 — Threshold / Critical — 89-100% of CP · 20-90 min
  • VO2 Max — 100-120% of CP · 3-8 min intervals

Power is instantaneous — it tells you what you're doing right now, with no lag. HR tells you what your body is responding to, which is delayed by 30-60 seconds. Use both. When power and HR disagree, treat it as information — not a problem to solve. Heat, dehydration, poor sleep, or accumulated fatigue all show up as HR running high (or sometimes low) for a given power output. The right move depends on which signal the day belongs to.

Run Zones — % of CS

CS (Critical Speed) is the running analog of CP — the highest pace you can sustain in a quasi-steady state. CS is the run anchor, and pace is the cleanest field expression of sustainable run performance. Run zones are built from CS, and HR is used to verify the cost.

Canonical reference (live from ECHO):
  • ZR — Recovery — slower than 75% of CS pace · HR ≤ 76% LTHR · regeneration and reset
  • Z1 — Durability Base — 75-85% of CS pace · HR ~76-86% LTHR · primary low-cost run volume
  • Z2 — Steady / Durable — 85-95% of CS pace · HR 86-93% LTHR · steady, durability support, race-adjacent
  • Z3 — Threshold / Critical — 95-105% of CS pace · HR 93-100% LTHR · threshold / critical-speed work
  • VO2 Max — 105-120% of CS pace · HR lagging confirmation only · 3-8 min interval work

Reminder: run pace is time-per-distance (e.g. 4:30/km), so higher % of CS pace = slower pace. Only VO2 Max is faster than CS itself.

Run power. If you train with a run power meter, run power should mirror CS, not replace it. CS is the field-tested reality; watts help you execute that reality on hills and uneven terrain. When watts and CS disagree, trust the field-tested CS anchor first — then use watts as a secondary execution aid. On hot days, hills, or off-road, HR and RPE will be more honest than pace.

Swim Zones — % of CS (swim)

Swim CS is the pool analog of CP and run CS — the highest swim pace you can hold in a quasi-steady state. Testing protocols (multiple distances, e.g. 200 / 400 / 800 or 200 / 400 / 1500) live in Section 3.

Swim zones compress around CS more tightly than bike or run because of how sensitive swimming is to technique. The percentage bands look small — that's correct.

Canonical reference (live from ECHO):
  • ZR — Recovery — 90-95% of CS · unlimited duration · easy technique work
  • Z1 — Durability Base — 95-97% of CS · 45-90 min · long aerobic swim volume
  • Z2 — Steady / Durable — 97-99% of CS · 30-60 min · sustained aerobic, race-pace for long course
  • Z3 — Threshold / Critical — 99-102% of CS · 20-40 min · threshold / critical-speed swim work
  • Above CS — intervals only:
    • Repeatable: CS + 5% — e.g. 6-8 × 200m
    • Short max: CS + 10% — e.g. 4-8 × 25-100m

Hold the Water

Swim intensity is governed by technique before it is governed by effort. You can muscle through a hard bike or run set — you cannot muscle through swim intensity without losing the catch, the rhythm, and the energy economy that make swimming work.

The phrase we use is Hold the Water. Feel the pressure on your forearm and palm through the entire pull, on every stroke. If a set forces you to lose your hold, the prescribed intensity was too high for that day — slow down and keep the technique intact. Speed in the water comes from a longer, cleaner stroke, not a more violent one.

This is why swim zones compress around CS the way they do, and why above-CS work is delivered as discrete intervals rather than sustained efforts. Hold the water first, then add pressure.

RPE — the verification and safety valve

RPE is not a primary zone system in ECHO. It's the verification layer: a way to check whether the prescribed output is actually affordable for the athlete on the day.

The rule: RPE outranks the anchor when internal strain spikes. If the output target says 250W and the athlete reports RPE 10/10 on what should be a Z2 day, the day is wrong, not the math. Pull back the output, finish the session at an honest cost, and figure out the why afterward (heat? illness? sleep debt? fueling? life stress?).

That's the safety-valve job RPE does. Every athlete should be in the habit of rating sessions in plain language — "easy," "steady," "hard," "very hard," "maxed out" — and reporting it. The number itself matters less than the trend over time relative to the prescribed work.

How they fit together — Anchor → Range → Verify

  • 1. Anchor — test the output anchor (CP for bike, CS for run and swim, LTHR for HR). Use the best anchor available; fallbacks only when a real test isn't possible.
  • 2. Range — build ZR / Z1 / Z2 / Z3 / VO2 Max from the anchor. Do not blur the edges to match athlete preference.
  • 3. Verify — check internal cost with HR drift, breathing, and RPE. If the verification doesn't match the anchor, that's information.
  • 4. Apply context — heat, terrain, fatigue, and fueling can override. Zones are anchors, not permission to ignore reality.
